Russia at the turn of the twentieth century held two empires of probability theory, separated by the Pulkovo meridian and a long-running dispute over what mathematics was permitted to claim about human action. On one side stood St. Petersburg University, the secular, republican heir to a mathematical lineage stretching from Jakob Bernoulli's 1713 *Ars Conjectandi* through the rigorous foundational work of Pafnuty Chebyshev. On the other stood Moscow University, an ecclesiastical stronghold whose faculty was closely aligned with the Russian Orthodox Church. 

The spark for what would become the mathematical foundation of language modeling was struck in Moscow. Pavel Alekseevich Nekrasov, a mathematician on the Moscow faculty who had begun his education at a theological seminary, published a paper in 1902. Nekrasov's claim, according to Andrei Andreevich Markov's interpretation, was that the pairwise independence of summands was both necessary and sufficient for the Weak Law of Large Numbers to hold. The implication of this argument was sweeping. As paraphrased by the science writer Brian Hayes, the popular form of the argument ran like this: voluntary acts are independent, like coin flips; the law of large numbers applies only to such independent events; data gathered by social scientists, such as crime statistics, conform to the law of large numbers; therefore, the underlying acts of individuals must be independent and voluntary. Free will, in Nekrasov's formulation, was mathematically demonstrable.

From St. Petersburg, the forty-nine-year-old Markov read the argument as a fundamental misuse of probability theory. Markov, born in Ryazan in 1856, was by most accounts a nettlesome character, abrasive even with friends and fiercely combative with rivals. In private correspondence to the statistician A. A. Chuprov, Markov called Nekrasov's work "an abuse of mathematics." He was not a man given to measured opposition. When Tsar Nicholas II vetoed the writer Maxim Gorky's election to the Imperial Academy of Sciences in 1902, Markov announced he would refuse all future tsarist honors. When the Russian Orthodox Church excommunicated Leo Tolstoy in 1908, Markov asked the Church to excommunicate him as well, a request that was granted. Nekrasov's attempt to fuse mathematics with theology offended Markov's deepest republican and scientific convictions.

Markov had retired from St. Petersburg University as Emeritus Professor in 1905, though he remained an Academician and continued to teach the probability course he had inherited from Chebyshev in 1883. It was from this position that he formulated his response. In a 1906 paper for the Kazan *Izvestiia*, Markov constructed a deliberate counterexample to Nekrasov's claim. He proved that the Weak Law of Large Numbers held for a two-state chain in which all four transition probabilities lay strictly between zero and one. If the current state was A, the next state would be A with probability p1 and B with probability 1-p1, and the law of large numbers would still apply despite the dependence between successive trials. 

The proof was symbolic, written on paper, and ran entirely without empirical illustration. It was the beginning of a six-year, eight-paper purely theoretical series extending chain dependence theory to multiple states, complex chains, and events left without observation. These follow-ups appeared in Russian regional bulletins, the *Bulletin de l'Académie Impériale des Sciences de St.-Pétersbourg*, and eventually reached a French-language readership via *Acta Mathematica* in 1910. Across all of these papers, Markov refused on principle to look for empirical applications. In the words of the historian David Link, during those six years, Markov could not think of a practical illustration of his deductions, or was simply not interested in finding one. Markov himself was explicit about his motivations. In a letter to Chuprov, he wrote that he was concerned only with questions of pure analysis, adding that he referred to the question of the applicability of probability theory with indifference.

This indifference broke on January 23, 1913, according to the Old Style calendar. While Russia at large prepared to celebrate three hundred years of Romanov rule, Markov organized a lecture for the physical-mathematical faculty of the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg. He had chosen to commemorate a different anniversary: the bicentenary of Jakob Bernoulli's *Ars Conjectandi*, the 1713 posthumous publication that first formulated the law of large numbers. This choice lent the physical-mathematical faculty's event a clear political dimension against the imperial tercentenary. Standing before his colleagues, Markov finally provided an empirical illustration of the theorem he had proved in 1906. 

His test set was literary. He had taken the first 20,000 letters of Aleksandr Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin*—the entire first chapter and sixteen stanzas of the second—and stripped out the punctuation, the spaces, the hard signs (ъ), and the soft signs (ь), noting that the latter were not pronounced independently but merely modified the preceding letter. He organized this unbroken stream of Cyrillic characters into two hundred small tables, each containing ten rows by ten columns. He then counted the vowels in each column, paired columns one and six, two and seven, three and eight, four and nine, and five and ten, and entered the sums in bold. This layout permitted simultaneous horizontal and vertical aggregation across each table. 

The arithmetic mean number of vowels per hundred-letter block was calculated as 43.19. From this, Markov derived the unconditional probability that a random letter is a vowel as approximately 0.432. He then computed the variance using the method of least squares, established by Adrien-Marie Legendre in 1805 and Carl Friedrich Gauss in 1809. The sum of squared deviations across the 200 blocks was 1022.8, giving a variance of 5.114. 

But Markov did not stop at independent probabilities. Returning to the unbroken stream of letters, he counted the pair patterns. Out of 8,638 total vowels, he found 1,104 vowel-vowel pairs. Thus, the probability of a vowel given that the previous letter was a vowel—what Markov denoted as p1—was 0.128. Accounting for the fact that the first letter has no predecessor, he found 7,534 consonants following a vowel, leaving 3,827 consonant-consonant pairs out of 11,361 available consonant positions. The probability of a vowel following a consonant, p0, was therefore 0.663. The probability of a consonant following a vowel, denoted q1, was 0.872. 

He then counted the triple patterns to examine the second-order chain. He found 115 vowel-vowel-vowel sequences and 505 consonant-consonant-consonant sequences. After two vowels in a row, the probability of a third vowel collapsed to 0.104. After two consonants, the probability of another consonant was 0.132. When these figures were plugged into a two-step chain, the theoretical dispersion coefficient was 0.195. This was a much tighter fit to the empirical dispersion coefficient of 0.208 than the 0.300 coefficient yielded by the one-step chain. The chain of dependence was real, and it demonstrably extended at least one letter back. The labor required for this pencil-and-paper tabulation was immense; Brian Hayes, attempting a similar count a century later on a translated text, estimated that Markov must have spent several days on these tables. 

Markov's *Onegin* analysis was published in the *Bulletin of the Imperial Academy of Sciences of St. Petersburg* in Russian only. A few years later, he published a second statistical analysis of 100,000 letters from S. T. Aksakov's autobiographical novel *Childhood Years of Bagrov's Grandson*. This parallel exercise was included in the appendix to the posthumous 1924 fourth edition of his *Calculus of Probability*. By the time Markov died in Petrograd in 1922, and Nekrasov followed in 1924, their theological and mathematical dispute had already begun to fall into post-revolutionary obscurity.

Markov's chains, however, traveled west. They did so not through Markov's own papers, but through the term-of-art "Markoff process." A German translation of Markov's textbook by Heinrich Liebmann in 1912 seeded the concept in the European mathematical community. Maurice Fréchet generalized the formalism in his 1938 Paris publication, *Méthode des fonctions arbitraires*. Andrei Kolmogorov's 1933 *Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung* recast probability, including Markov chains, in measure-theoretic language. By 1948, the Markoff process was a settled mathematical term with an established literature, even as the *Onegin* paper itself remained unread outside Russia.

It was in 1948 that Claude Shannon published "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" in the *Bell System Technical Journal*. In the third section of his paper, "The Series of Approximations to English," Shannon adopted discrete Markoff processes as the model of an information source. He presented worked first-, second-, third-order, and word-level n-gram approximations to English text. The second-order output read "OCRO HLI RGWR NMIELWIS EU LL NBNESEBYA TH EEI ALHENHTTPA OOBTTVA NAH BRL," while the third-order produced "IN NO IST LAT WHEY CRATICT FROURE BIRS GROCID PONDENOME OF DEMONSTURES OF THE REPTAGIN IS REGOACTIONA OF CRE." 

Shannon generalized the construction in his fourth section, "Graphical Representation of a Markoff Process." Crucially, his footnote routing readers to a detailed treatment of the subject pointed not to Markov, but to Fréchet's 1938 work. The transmission was conceptual, moving via the genus-name of the process rather than direct citation of the 1913 or 1906 papers. Furthermore, Shannon's text approximations were themselves a hand-counting exercise. As Shannon detailed in section three, his second-order sample was constructed using a book of random numbers in conjunction with a table of letter frequencies. The higher-order examples were generated by opening a book at random, finding the previous letters, and copying the next one. The labor involved, Shannon noted, became enormous at the next stage.

Markov's *Onegin* paper remained inaccessible to the English-speaking world until 1955, when Morris Halle, an MIT linguist, produced a mimeographed English translation at the request of colleagues exploring statistical approaches to language. That translation remained unpublished, surviving only in a few libraries until David Link and his colleagues published a widely available English version in the journal *Science in Context* in 2006. 

What lay between Markov's pencil-and-paper Pushkin tables and any practical use of n-gram models was four decades and an institutional infrastructure that did not yet exist. There was no published English translation of the *Onegin* paper; there was no theory of communication that needed a stochastic source; and there was no digital storage capable of carrying transition matrices for any vocabulary larger than two states. The practical questions modern Markov-chain research cares about—convergence speed, error bounds for prematurely terminated simulations—are questions only a computer can answer, and Markov never had one.

Markov did not foresee text generation. He proved a theorem against a theological misuse of mathematics, gave it a literary demonstration, and walked away. The chain of transmission to twenty-first-century language modeling is real, but it is deeply indirect. It would take a Cold War theory of communication (as will be explored in Chapter 9) and the eventual emergence of the statistical underground (Chapter 30) to turn a mathematical counterexample into the foundation of a new artificial intelligence.
