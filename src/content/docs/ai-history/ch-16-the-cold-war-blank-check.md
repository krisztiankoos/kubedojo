The story of how artificial intelligence secured its first massive wave of institutional funding begins not with computers, but with a sudden crisis in the sky. In October 1957, the Soviet Union successfully launched Sputnik, the first artificial satellite. The launch shattered American confidence and triggered a deep, systemic anxiety in Washington over the prospect of falling behind in science and technology. This shock led directly to the creation of the Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) on February 7, 1958. From its inception, the agency was animated by a retrospective mission of preventing another technological surprise and preserving the technological superiority of the United States.

However, ARPA did not immediately turn to artificial intelligence. Instead, the defense establishment recognized that its most severe vulnerability was the slow, rigid nature of its command and control infrastructure. The sheer speed of modern warfare meant that human decision-makers risked being overwhelmed by incoming data. Between June and November 1961, studies conducted by the Institute for Defense Analyses for the Department of Defense identified computer-related research as the crucial avenue for improving these command-and-control systems. It was within this context of managing national security crises that the Information Processing Techniques Office (IPTO) was formed in 1962.

The conceptual vocabulary that would eventually open the defense budget to symbolic computing was provided by J. C. R. Licklider. In March 1960, Licklider published a seminal paper titled "Man-Computer Symbiosis," which outlined a bold vision of close, cooperative interaction between humans and computers. He argued that for humans and machines to think together to solve complex problems, several technical prerequisites had to be met. His framework explicitly named developments in computer time-sharing, memory components, memory organization, programming languages, and input and output equipment as the necessary foundation.

Licklider’s vision offered a powerful translation: interactive computing was not merely an abstract scientific pursuit, but the vital infrastructure for future military decision-making. As historian Paul N. Edwards has argued, artificial intelligence essentially piggybacked into ARPA through these interactive command-and-control concerns. When Licklider became the first director of IPTO in October 1962, he carried this philosophy into government. He utilized rigorous criteria to select projects, demanding scientific excellence, prospects for relevance to Department of Defense problems, and a coherent relationship across sponsored efforts. The money that would flow to early artificial intelligence researchers was permissible precisely because the military problem it aimed to solve—keeping command, control, and intelligence from being surprised—was deliberately broad.

To understand the magnitude of IPTO's impact, it is necessary to look at the baseline of artificial intelligence research before the agency began funding at scale. Government money was already underwriting early symbolic reasoning work, but it was fragmented and modest, scattered across defense-adjacent institutions. The baseline of government support was present, but it lacked coherence and scale.

At the RAND Corporation, for instance, Allen Newell, Herbert A. Simon, and J. C. Shaw had developed the Logic Theorist, an early milestone demonstrating that a machine could prove mathematical theorems. This work was funded almost entirely by the Air Force through Project RAND. Furthermore, the programming was executed on the JOHNNIAC computer, a machine that was itself funded by the Air Force. Newell’s 1957 paper describing the Logic Theory Machine revealed that dealing with a non-numerical problem class required variable storage and an interpretive pseudo-code language. It proved that symbolic theorem proving was fundamentally constrained by the architecture of machine memory and programming languages.

Newell and Simon continued this trajectory at the Graduate School of Industrial Administration at Carnegie Tech, developing the General Problem Solver. Yet, until the early 1960s, this work remained principally funded by a patchwork of Air Force and Office of Naval Research money.

A similar environment existed at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. In September 1957, John McCarthy and Marvin Minsky established the MIT Artificial Intelligence Project. Despite the project’s future prominence, its early days were defined by a profound lack of resources. Supported through military-funded arrangements via the Research Laboratory of Electronics, the project consisted of little more than a single room, two programmers, a secretary, and a keypunch machine. The intellectual ambition of early symbolic artificial intelligence was already present, but the field lacked the computing power, the institutional coherence, and the sheer capital required to grow. Defense funding existed, but it had not yet gathered into a centralized force.

The establishment of IPTO under Licklider radically changed the scale of artificial intelligence research, transforming it from a collection of small, isolated efforts into a large, high-profile academic domain. From the 1960s onward, the defense agency provided the bulk of United States support for artificial intelligence, an influx of resources that helped legitimize it as a permanent discipline.

Licklider managed IPTO and the Behavioral Sciences Office with a combined budget of about $11 million, operating with a surprisingly lean administrative structure. His approach represented a departure from typical military procurement. Rather than scattering small grants to individual researchers for narrow deliverables, IPTO funded coherent programs and established major centers of excellence. Early centers were anchored at MIT and Carnegie Tech, reflecting Licklider's preference for broad institutional commitments over isolated experiments.

At MIT, Project MAC became one of IPTO's first major investments. The project was conceived as an experiment in interactive computing, aimed broadly at human-computer interaction and general-purpose time-sharing. Licklider was deeply involved in the evaluation of the proposal, and the defense agency endorsed a $3-million contract within days.

At Carnegie Tech, a proposal bolstered by the combined presence of Simon, Newell, Alan Perlis, and Herbert Green received similar backing. Licklider wanted to strengthen their ongoing information-processing work, later identifying the institution as an essential early center of excellence.

Stanford soon emerged as a third major center after John McCarthy relocated there. According to the laboratory's own retrospective, the Stanford Artificial Intelligence Project obtained its initial ARPA support beginning on June 15, 1963. Over the next decade, the project expanded significantly, ordered a PDP-6 computer, and relocated to the D.C. Power Laboratory. By June 1973, about two-thirds of the laboratory's 128 members were at least partly supported by the agency. A subsequent center eventually emerged at the Stanford Research Institute, which, according to summaries of its early contracts, focused its initial research on automata designed to operate in hostile environments.

While this era of funding is sometimes described retrospectively as a "blank check," that phrase functions better as a metaphor for the open-ended nature of the research than as a literal description of government contracts. Licklider and his successors maintained a coherent strategy that aligned with command-and-control objectives. IPTO’s direction actively shaped local decisions, sometimes forcing researchers to share resources rather than building independent silos. For example, when McCarthy requested an independent time-sharing system for Stanford, IPTO refused. Instead, the agency funded the development of a LISP programming system for the System Development Corporation's Q-32 computer, a West Coast machine that multiple researchers could access. Furthermore, Licklider instituted regular Principal Investigator meetings, using them as mechanisms for exchange, evaluation, and community building across the domains of time-sharing, networking, graphics, and artificial intelligence. The community itself became part of the infrastructure.

The permissive funding provided by IPTO did not buy magic; it bought physical and software infrastructure. The shift from theoretical proposals to operational symbolic systems required interactive access, immense memory by the standards of the day, symbolic languages, and rooms full of terminals where graduate communities could live inside the systems they were building.

The goals of these major laboratories were explicitly tied to this hardware reality. Project MAC’s 1964–1965 progress report framed its core objective as the pursuit of online computing for individual intellectual work, sustained by a large multiple-access system. The Compatible Time-Sharing System, running on an IBM 7094, served dual purposes: it functioned simultaneously as a reliable service facility and as an active laboratory for man-machine interaction.

By the 1968–1969 period, the MIT Artificial Intelligence section was documenting an advanced ecosystem built entirely upon defense-funded hardware. Their environment relied heavily on PDP-6 and PDP-10 computers running the Incompatible Timesharing System. The money had also purchased the development of symbolic manipulation infrastructure like MATHLAB and MACLISP, alongside the hardware needed for real-time device control, including robotic hands and computer eyes.

The dependence on this shared infrastructure meant that hardware limitations routinely dictated the direction of theoretical research. At the System Development Corporation, the Q-32 computer proved to have too little memory for the planned implementation of the LISP 2 language. As John McCarthy later recalled, these physical resource constraints forced the developers to abandon the Q-32 and pivot their targets toward the IBM 360/67 architecture and the PDP-6 and PDP-10 machines. The ambition of early artificial intelligence was inexorably bound to the specific machines that IPTO was willing to buy and maintain.

By the late 1960s, the political and administrative climate surrounding defense research began to change. The permissive bargain of the early Cold War—where open-ended basic research was tolerated under the broad umbrella of command and control—started to require much more explicit justification.

This shift was visible within the agency's own documentation. During fiscal year 1965, IPTO program areas were described with broad labels such as command-and-control processes, human-machine interaction, computer languages, programming techniques, time-sharing, and advanced software. However, as Edwards has noted, artificial intelligence did not appear as a separate line item in the agency's budgets until 1968. By fiscal year 1971, IPTO's program tables had evolved to explicitly include categories like "Intelligent systems," "Speech understanding systems," "Distributed information systems," and "Computer networks." The discipline had become a named, trackable commodity.

Simultaneously, the United States Congress imposed stricter boundaries on military research spending. In November 1969, Public Law 91-121 was enacted. Section 203 of the statute—widely known as the Mansfield Amendment—mandated that no defense research funds could be used during the 1970 fiscal year unless the project or study possessed a "direct and apparent relationship to a specific military function or operation."

The statute did not act as a sudden winter that ended artificial intelligence funding overnight. The agency's support for major centers continued, but the rhetorical room for pure, open-ended scientific inquiry narrowed. The law forced a new reality onto program managers and researchers alike: the money was still there, but the broad metaphor of human-computer symbiosis had to be increasingly translated into the rigid language of specific military applications. The era of the Cold War's most unconstrained patronage had begun to tighten its rules.
