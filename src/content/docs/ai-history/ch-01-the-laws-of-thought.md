George Boole was born on November 2, 1815, in the small cathedral city of Lincoln, Lincolnshire, England. His father was a tradesman of very limited means whose true and serious avocations were the pursuit of mathematics and the construction of optical instruments. In a household where formal academic pathways were financially out of reach, the elder Boole instructed his son in elementary mathematics and the use of optical devices. A friendly Lincoln bookseller helped the boy with Latin grammar, but beyond these initial foundations, the younger Boole was entirely self-taught. At the age of twelve, his metrical translation of an ode of Horace was published by his proud father in a local Lincoln journal, an achievement that prompted a neighbouring schoolmaster to write in and publicly deny that a translation of such quality could have been produced by one so young.

Between the ages of sixteen and twenty, Boole worked as an assistant teacher, first at Doncaster in Yorkshire and then at Waddington near Lincoln. He devoted his sparse leisure hours to the study of modern languages and patristic literature, teaching himself Greek, German, and French. At the age of twenty, in order to support his ageing parents, he opened his own school in Lincoln. Amidst the daily demands of school-teaching, Boole's intellectual focus shifted firmly back to mathematics. Working alone in the original French, he began to study Pierre-Simon Laplace's *Mécanique céleste* and Joseph-Louis Lagrange's *Mécanique analytique*. Out of the meticulous notes he took while working through Lagrange, his first mathematical memoir began to take shape.

He soon found an advocate in Duncan F. Gregory, the founding editor of the *Cambridge Mathematical Journal*, who helped the provincial schoolmaster bring his early work into print. Gregory and others in the Cambridge circle recognised Boole's talent and suggested he take the regular mathematical course at the university. Boole declined; he could not afford to leave his parents or the school that sustained them. Working entirely outside the university system, he submitted a memoir on a general method in analysis—applying the algebraic "separation of symbols" technique to differential equations—which was published in 1844 in the *Transactions of the Royal Society of London*. The paper earned him a Royal Medal. Three years later, in 1847, he published a slim volume titled *The Mathematical Analysis of Logic*, his first foray into the algebra of reasoning.

In 1849, Boole's life was transformed when he was appointed to the chair of mathematics at the newly founded Queen's College, Cork, in Ireland. The non-denominational college offered Boole an institutional home, a regular salary, and the time he had never possessed in Lincoln. There, in late 1853, sitting at his desk at 5, Grenville-place, Cork, Boole signed the preface to the book he had been preparing for years.

In 1854, the London firm of Walton & Maberly published *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, on which are founded the Mathematical Theories of Logic and Probabilities*. It was dedicated to Dr. John Ryall, the Vice-President and Professor of Greek at Queen's College, Cork. In its opening chapter, Boole laid out an ambitious, unifying design statement: "to investigate the fundamental laws of those operations of the mind by which reasoning is performed; to give expression to them in the symbolical language of a Calculus."

In the second chapter of the book, Boole established the infrastructure of his system on the printed page. He proposed that all operations of language as an instrument of reasoning could be conducted by a system of signs composed of three elements. First were the literal symbols, such as $x$ and $y$, representing things or classes of things. Second were the signs of operation—$+$, $-$, and $\times$—by which these conceptions could be combined or resolved. Third was the sign of identity, $=$.

Boole demonstrated the move from a linguistic name to a formal class using what would become his most famous worked example. "Let $x$ represent 'all men,' or the class 'men,'" he wrote. If $x$ alone stands for 'white things,' and $y$ stands for 'sheep,' then let $xy$ stand for 'white sheep.' This operation of multiplication represented the logical intersection of two classes, gathering only the objects to which both names apply. Boole swiftly derived that the multiplication of these literal symbols is commutative: $xy = yx$. The order in which the mind conceptually combines "white" and "sheep" does not change the resulting class.

When he turned to addition, however, Boole made a restriction that would define the limits of his original system. He insisted that the words 'and' and 'or,' when interposed between terms descriptive of classes, imply in strictness that those classes are completely distinct, so that no member of one is found in another. Consequently, in Boole's 1854 calculus, the addition $x + y$ was a *partial* operation. The equation $x + y = y + x$ was valid, but only when the classes were mutually exclusive. Boole's symbol $+$ was not the modern inclusive-OR; it was strictly disjoint.

With the elements defined, Boole proceeded in Chapter III, "Derivation of the Laws," to assign mathematical limits to his logical space. He observed that of all numerical symbols, only two satisfied the laws he was discovering. He assigned these two symbols, 0 and 1, to the boundaries of class extension: "the respective interpretations of the symbols 0 and 1 in the system of Logic are Nothing and Universe." If $x$ represents any class of objects, Boole defined the logical complement as $1 - x$, which he called "the contrary or supplementary class of objects, i.e. the class including all objects which are not comprehended in the class $x$."

From these definitions emerged the cornerstone of the entire calculus. If the class $x$ is multiplied by its contrary $1 - x$, the result must be empty, because an object cannot both belong to a class and not belong to it. Algebraically, $x(1 - x) = 0$, which expands to $x^2 = x$. Boole identified this fundamental law of thought as the algebraic expression of the Aristotelian principle of contradiction. To prove the lineage, he quoted Aristotle's *Metaphysica* directly in Greek in a footnote: "It is impossible that the same quality should both belong and not belong to the same thing."

Boole termed the equation $x^2 = x$ the "law of duality." (It would not receive its modern name, the "idempotent law," until the Harvard mathematician Benjamin Peirce renamed it in 1870.) More importantly, Boole argued that the mathematical form of this equation revealed a deep truth about human cognition. "It is a consequence of the fact that the fundamental equation of thought is of the second degree," Boole wrote, "that we perform the operation of analysis and classification, by division into pairs of opposites, or, as it is technically said, by dichotomy." This was Boole's own justification for a two-valued logic: because the fundamental algebraic equation of reasoning is a quadratic, the natural mode of human analysis is inherently binary.

By the end of Chapter III, Boole had successfully built a calculus of two values, three operations, and one fundamental law. Yet his strict-disjoint addition remained a limitation. Boole himself was aware of the need to express inclusive, overlapping choices. In Chapter IV, he provided a manual workaround for the non-disjoint case, giving the exact algebraic construction $x + y(1 - x)$ for use when the classes denoted by $x$ and $y$ are not exclusive. The mathematical form of the inclusive-OR was thus present in Boole's own text, but he chose not to redefine his fundamental $+$ symbol to accommodate it.

Boole also demonstrated that his algebra was not limited merely to the classification of objects. While the early chapters of *Laws of Thought* (Chapters II through X) deal with "primary propositions"—what we now call class algebra—the subsequent chapters (Chapters XI and XII) apply the exact same calculus to "secondary propositions," or propositions about propositions. This was Boole's propositional logic. The modern framing that Boole only invented a class algebra, and that computer scientists later had to independently rediscover propositional logic, is wrong by omission; both systems exist side-by-side in the 1854 text.

The leap from Boole's partial-algebra to modern Boolean algebra occurred ten years later, driven by the logician W. S. Jevons. In 1864, Jevons wrote to Boole proposing a radical simplification: that the $+$ symbol should be redefined as inclusive disjunction, meaning $x + x = x$. Boole flatly rejected the change, replying in correspondence that "it is not true that in Logic $x + x = x$." Undeterred, Jevons abandoned Boole's partial-addition framework entirely, publishing a system that established the first version of modern Boolean algebra. The formal logic that underpins modern computer science descends through this historical hinge—from Jevons, through C. S. Peirce and Ernst Schröder—and not directly from Boole's 1854 book unmodified.

By the time Jevons's revision began to circulate, Boole's life was drawing to a sudden close. In 1855, a year after the publication of *Laws of Thought*, Boole had married Mary Everest. She was the niece of Dr. John Ryall, the colleague to whom Boole had dedicated his book, and also the niece of Sir George Everest, the former Surveyor General of India. The couple settled in Cork and had five daughters together. In 1857, Boole's mathematical achievements were formally recognised when he was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society.

In late November 1864 (a date sometimes cited specifically as November 24), Boole walked from his residence to Queen's College Cork in heavy rain. (Biographical accounts vary slightly, describing the distance as either two or three miles.) Arriving soaked, he proceeded to deliver his lecture in his wet clothes. The severe chill he contracted soon developed into a "feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs," leading to a fatal case of pneumonia. George Boole died at Ballintemple, County Cork, on December 8, 1864, at the age of 49.

In the decades following his death, a popular legend emerged that Mary Everest Boole dedicated her life to preserving her husband's mathematical legacy and keeping his revolutionary logical theories in academic circulation. However, the verified open-access historical record tells a different, though equally remarkable, story. With the support of the theologian Frederick Denison Maurice, Mary Everest Boole became a librarian at Queen's College, London. She became a prominent educator, teaching mathematics to women and children using methods derived from her own early tutoring. She invented "curve stitching," or string geometry, as a pedagogical tool, and from the 1880s onward published prolifically on the philosophy of mathematical education. The framing that she preserved George Boole's symbolic logic is folk history; her own documented legacy lies in mathematical pedagogy, not formal algebraic logic.

George Boole left behind a calculus of classes and propositions, etched entirely in paper and ink. He framed his project explicitly as a science of the mind, not as an engineering blueprint. At no point in *Laws of Thought* did he propose the construction of physical machinery to execute his equations, nor does the text contain the seed of a mechanical design. It would take nearly eighty years for a new generation of engineers to recognize that the algebra of human reasoning, built to operate on the values of Universe and Nothing, could be perfectly mapped onto the physical flow of electrical current.
