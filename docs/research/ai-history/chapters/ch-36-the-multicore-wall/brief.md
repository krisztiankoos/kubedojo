# Brief: Chapter 36 — The Multicore Wall

## Thesis

The free ride that single-thread CPUs gave software for thirty years did not end with one dramatic invention; it ended with one quiet cancellation. On May 7, 2004, Intel killed the Tejas Pentium 4 and the Jayhawk Xeon and reorganized its 2005 desktop and server roadmaps around dual-core dies. The cancellation was not driven by a competitive threat — Tejas had no shipping rival — it was driven by the same heat budget that Shekhar Borkar had warned about in 1999 and that Kunle Olukotun's group at Stanford had argued for an alternative to in 1996. Dennard scaling — the four-decade-old IBM recipe under which every transistor generation got 30% smaller, 40% faster, and consumed proportionally less power — had finally stopped paying its full bill. By December 2004 Herb Sutter had named the consequence ("The Free Lunch Is Over"); by December 2006 the Berkeley View report had named the diagnosis ("Power Wall + Memory Wall + ILP Wall = Brick Wall"). The chapter's frame: the multicore pivot was not the *birth* of parallelism (vector machines, SMP, and the Stanford Hydra prototype all predate it). It was the moment the mainstream desktop CPU stopped being a single fast scalar processor. That redefinition — every laptop now ships a small parallel computer — is the infrastructural condition under which the next decade of AI compute scaling becomes possible.

## Scope

- **IN SCOPE:** the late-1990s clock-speed race and Intel's NetBurst architecture; the early warnings (Borkar 1999, Olukotun et al. 1996); the May 7, 2004 cancellation of Tejas and Jayhawk and Intel's announced acceleration of a dual-core 2005 roadmap; Sutter's December 2004 / March 2005 "Free Lunch" essay and what it claimed software would have to do; the 2005 shipment of Pentium D and Athlon 64 X2 as the first mainstream x86 dual-core desktop chips; the Berkeley View 2006 retrospective framing ("Brick Wall," "from sequential to parallel").
- **OUT OF SCOPE:** GPUs, CUDA, and accelerator-driven AI compute (Part 7 — Ch41 *The Graphics Hack*, Ch42 *CUDA*); custom AI silicon (Ch49); the deep-learning compute scaling story (Ch43+); the prior Cray/MPP supercomputing parallelism tradition (background only, no scenes); IBM POWER4 and Sun Niagara, which the chapter cites only as the existing-server-multicore precedents Berkeley View names — they get one paragraph, not a scene.

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not present multicore as an AI invention or even as primarily an AI consequence. The May 2004 cancellation was about server power-density economics, not neural networks; nobody at Intel in 2004 was reorganizing the roadmap because of ImageNet. The chapter must also not present the multicore pivot as sudden. Olukotun's group made the explicit "single-chip multiprocessor" case in 1996; Borkar warned about the power-density wall at IEEE Micro in 1999; IBM shipped the dual-core POWER4 in 2001; Sun was committed to Niagara before Tejas died. What Tejas's cancellation marked was the moment the *mass-market x86* line — the chips inside almost every consumer laptop and the volume of the server market — gave up trying to scale single-thread frequency. The chapter must also not anticipate Part 7. It can name the through-line ("a world that has just learned to build software for many cores will, ten years later, be ready to build software for thousands of GPU cores") but must not relitigate the GPU story here.

The chapter must also avoid two specific factual overclaims that the existing legacy prose makes and that the Sutter and Borkar/Chien sources do not support: (1) that Tejas was specifically a "4 GHz" chip — it was not; the cancelled Tejas samples ran at 2.8 GHz at ~150 W, and the "4 GHz" target was a *Pentium 4 line* target Intel formally abandoned in fall 2004 (Sutter, "Obstacles" section); and (2) that Borkar's paper compared chips to "the surface of a nuclear reactor" — that comparison comes from a different and slightly later Patrick Gelsinger keynote graphic and should not be attributed to Borkar 1999.

The chapter must not invent dialogue or internal motives for any participant. Every quoted sentence must come from a verified primary source. Reconstructed atmospherics are permitted only where multiple independent sources converge.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Gigahertz Religion (1999–2003).** Intel's NetBurst architecture (introduced 2000) was explicitly designed around a deep pipeline so that frequency could keep climbing; consumers learned to read CPU performance off the GHz number on the box. Behind the scenes, Borkar (Intel) was warning at IEEE Micro in 1999 that the same scaling that delivered the GHz race was going to make the next generation thermally unmanageable, and Olukotun's group had already argued at ASPLOS 1996 that the architectural future was many small cores, not one big one. The industry kept climbing anyway.
2. **The Quiet Cancellation (May 7, 2004).** Intel confirms — late on a Friday, to *The Register* and other outlets — that Tejas (Pentium 4 successor) and Jayhawk (Xeon successor) are dead. The reason given on the record is single-core economics: "single core chips just don't do it for the company anymore" (Vance, *Register*, 2004-05-07). The dual-core schedule is moved 12–18 months earlier.
3. **The Free Lunch Is Over (Dec 2004 – Mar 2005).** Herb Sutter's *Dr. Dobb's Journal* article names what just happened, in software terms. "CPU performance growth as we have known it hit a wall two years ago. Most people have only recently started to notice." The article identifies three physical issues — heat, power consumption, current leakage — and lays out the three new growth axes (hyperthreading, multicore, cache). Critically it tells readers: "starting today the buffet will only be serving that one entrée and that one dessert" — concurrency.
4. **The Mainstream Pivot (2005).** Intel ships the Pentium D and AMD ships the Athlon 64 X2; both are mainstream x86 dual-core desktop parts and both arrive on the schedule Intel had promised one year earlier when killing Tejas. Sutter, writing in March 2005, documents this in real time: "multicore is available on current PowerPC and Sparc IV processors, and is coming in 2005 from Intel and AMD."
5. **Naming the Wall (2006).** Asanovic et al.'s *View from Berkeley* report restates the cancellation as a regime change: "Old CW: Increasing clock frequency is the primary method of improving processor performance. New CW: Increasing parallelism is the primary method." The report names the underlying constraints — Power Wall + Memory Wall + ILP Wall = Brick Wall — and explicitly cites both Borkar 1999 and the Tejas cancellation as evidence that "even representatives from Intel ... warned that traditional approaches to maximizing performance through maximizing clock speed have been pushed to their limit." The chapter closes by tying the multicore world that exists by 2006 to the parallel-software discipline that the next decade of AI compute will live inside without renegotiating.

## Conflict Notes

- **Tejas's intended clock speed.** The legacy prose calls Tejas "the 4 GHz chip Intel cancelled." The verified record is more nuanced: the *4 GHz Pentium 4 line target* was abandoned in fall 2004 (Sutter §"Obstacles ..."); the cancelled *Tejas samples* themselves were running at 2.8 GHz at ~150 W (Anandtech, 2004-01-09, *Wayback*). The chapter should phrase this as "Intel was about to push the Pentium 4 line past 4 GHz; the chip that was supposed to make that happen was cancelled" rather than "Intel cancelled its 4 GHz chip."
- **The "nuclear reactor" comparison.** Widely repeated in popular accounts of the multicore wall, often misattributed to Borkar 1999. The chapter should not use this comparison without primary anchor verification, which the team has not yet performed.
- **Whether the cancellation was thermal or single-core-economic.** The on-record Intel reason in 2004 (Vance, *Register*) was framed economically — "single core chips just don't do it for the company anymore" — and the technical reason (heat, leakage) was added by the secondary-source consensus that hardened in 2005–2006. The chapter should distinguish the two framings rather than collapse them.
- **Olukotun 1996 priority.** A purist reading of the architectural-history record (Olukotun et al. ASPLOS 1996) would argue that the multicore pivot was technically obvious for almost a decade before Intel acted on it. A purist reading of the manufacturing-economics record (Borkar 1999, Borkar & Chien 2011) would argue it could not have been acted on much earlier without giving up a generation of frequency lead to AMD. The chapter should present both readings, not pick one.
