# Timeline: Chapter 8 — The Stored Program

All dates in this timeline correspond to specific anchored claims in `sources.md`. Dates marked **Yellow** are widely-repeated secondary-source claims that lack a primary anchor Claude verified on 2026-04-28.

## 1942-1944: The ENIAC era

- **August 1942** *(Yellow)*: John Mauchly drafts a memorandum at the Moore School, "The Use of High Speed Vacuum Tube Devices for Calculating," proposing what becomes ENIAC. *Standard reference; primary anchor pending Penn archival access.*
- **9 April 1943**: Army Ordnance Department contract W-670-ORD-4926 between U.S. Army and the University of Pennsylvania authorizes the ENIAC project. *(Contract number Green-anchored via the title page of the First Draft, which carries forward the same contract number for the EDVAC follow-on work; date Yellow pending archival access.)*
- **1944** *(Yellow)*: John von Neumann joins the Moore School EDVAC project as a consultant after meeting Goldstine on a railway platform at Aberdeen Proving Ground. *Standard secondary-source claim; primary anchor pending Goldstine 1972 page reference.*
- **Summer 1944 - Spring 1945**: EDVAC design discussions inside the Moore School converge on storing instructions in writable electronic memory and on a small set of specialized organs. Eckert OH 13 pp.46-47 attributes the ideas primarily to Eckert, Mauchly, and the engineering team; Haigh 2014 p.5 acknowledges that Eckert and Mauchly "had created at least some of the ideas included in the First Draft." **Yellow** for sole-priority claims by either side.

## 1945: The First Draft

- **15 February 1945** *(Green for the year/month, Yellow for exact date)*: ENIAC dedication ceremony at the Moore School. *(Date cited via Wikipedia "Honeywell v. Sperry Rand" via Judge Larson's ruling — the dedication is part of the patent-bar timing argument. Primary anchor in the ruling text, which Claude has not directly fetched.)*
- **Late spring 1945**: Von Neumann completes a typescript that becomes the *First Draft*. The Moore School version of the typescript later survives in the Penn archives; an additional copy with §§13.6-13.7 survives in the American Philosophical Society Library, Philadelphia. *(Per the Godfrey 1992 introduction, edvac-IA.txt.)*
- **25 June 1945** *(Yellow)*: A first batch of 24 copies of the *First Draft* is reportedly distributed by Goldstine in advance of the formal release date. *Source: Wikipedia article on First Draft. Primary anchor pending.*
- **30 June 1945** **(Green)**: The *First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC*, "by John von Neumann," dated to the Moore School of Electrical Engineering at Penn under Contract W-670-ORD-4926, is finalized and circulated. The title page lists no other authors. *Source: First Draft (Godfrey TeX reset) title page, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28.*
- **30 September 1945** *(Yellow via secondary cite)*: Eckert and Mauchly file their own progress report on the EDVAC, "Automatic High Speed Computing: A Progress Report on the EDVAC," now in the ENIAC Patent Trial Collection, UPD 8.10, Univ. of Pennsylvania Archives and Records Center (Plaintiff Exhibit 3540 in *Honeywell v. Sperry Rand*). Cited via Haigh 2014 footnote 29.

## 1946: The IAS re-formalization and the credit dispute

- **15 February 1946** *(Yellow for date)*: Public unveiling of ENIAC at the Moore School. *Standard reference; primary anchor pending.*
- **March 1946** *(Yellow for date, Green for fact)*: Eckert and Mauchly resign from the Moore School over patent-assignment disputes. *Standard reference in Eckert OH 13 (date implicit but not directly extracted); primary anchor pending re-extraction of Eckert OH 13.*
- **8 July to 31 August 1946**: The Moore School Lectures (more formally, "Theory and Techniques for Design of Electronic Digital Computers") — a summer school at Penn that, per Haigh 2014 p.5, "Historians have credited the event as a crucial vector for the spread of the stored-program concept." The proceedings were later published as Campbell-Kelly and Williams, eds., *The Moore School Lectures: Theory and Techniques for Design of Electronic Digital Computers* (MIT Press, 1985).
- **28 June 1946** **(Green)**: Burks, Goldstine, and von Neumann issue *Preliminary Discussion of the Logical Design of an Electronic Computing Instrument* from the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton, under Contract W-36-034-ORD-7481. The report — three named authors, named co-authorship that the *First Draft* lacked — re-states the unified-memory rule cleanly in §1.3. *Source: Burks/Goldstine/von Neumann 1946, preface page; verified by Claude OCR 2026-04-28.*

## 1948-1949: First execution of stored programs

- **1948**: Richard Clippinger (a Ballistics Research Laboratory mathematician) contracts with the Moore School to retrofit ENIAC's function tables to hold a 60-instruction order code. Jean Jennings Bartik is hired to head the four-programmer team that implements the conversion. The team makes consulting trips to Princeton to discuss the instruction set with von Neumann. *Source: Bartik CHM oral history pp.31-33; verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28.*
- **21 June 1948**: The Manchester "Baby" (Manchester Small-Scale Experimental Machine) at the University of Manchester runs its first stored program. *Source: Haigh 2014 p.7.*
- **1948 (later)**: ENIAC, retrofitted with the 60 order code, runs a complex program in the modern code paradigm — including conditional branches, calculated-address data reads, and a subroutine called from more than one point in the code. *Source: Haigh 2014 p.4.*
- **6 May 1949**: Cambridge's EDSAC runs its first successful stored program. *Source: Haigh 2014 p.7.*
- **17 May 1949**: Nathaniel Rochester at IBM's Poughkeepsie facility writes an internal report titled "A Calculator Using Electrostatic Storage and a Stored Program" — the earliest located use of the phrase "stored program" in a written document. *Source: Haigh 2014 p.5; Haigh 2014 footnote 16 (IBM Corporate Archives, Somers, NY).*

## 1950s: Vocabulary settles, paradigms diverge

- **1953**: Willis Ware (RAND Corporation) writes simply of "what we know now as the 'stored program machine'" in a report on the IAS computer project, indicating that by 1953 the term was established within the small computer-research community. *Source: Haigh 2014 p.5.*
- **1950s** *(secondary)*: The phrase "stored program" enters general use, but only as a designation for a class of computers — not yet as the name of a "concept" worth historicizing.

## 1972-1985: Standard histories appear

- **1972**: Goldstine, *The Computer from Pascal to von Neumann* (Princeton UP) — the first major insider history. **Yellow** for all page anchors pending physical access.
- **1981**: Stern, *From ENIAC to UNIVAC: An Appraisal of the Eckert-Mauchly Computers* (Digital Press) — the first major Eckert/Mauchly-side history, written by the OH 13 interviewer. **Yellow**.
- **1985**: Williams, *A History of Computing Technology* (Prentice-Hall) — standard reference. **Yellow**.

## 1973: The legal afterlife

- **19 October 1973** **(Green for headline facts; Yellow for the four-grounds verbatim)**: *Honeywell, Inc. v. Sperry Rand Corp.*, 180 U.S.P.Q. 673, U.S. District Court for the District of Minnesota, Judge Earl R. Larson, rules the ENIAC patent invalid. One of the four grounds cited is that the *First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC* (June 30, 1945) constituted enabling disclosure prior to the patent application — the public-domain consequence Eckert had been worrying about for twenty-eight years. *Source: 180 U.S.P.Q. 673; cross-confirmed via Wikipedia and CBI archival finding aid (`archives.lib.umn.edu/repositories/3/resources/66`).*

## 1992-2014: Historiographic reframing

- **November 1992**: Michael D. Godfrey (Stanford Information Systems Laboratory) completes a TeX reset of the *First Draft* from the Moore School and APS Library typescripts. *Source: Godfrey introduction, edvac-IA.txt at the IA mirror; verified by Claude 2026-04-28.*
- **1993**: Godfrey and Hendry, "The Computer as von Neumann Planned It," *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing* 15(1), pp.11-21 — modern-language presentation of the *First Draft*'s logical design.
- **1993**: The *First Draft* is reprinted (in Godfrey's edited form, with §§13.6-13.7 and the model table from the APS copy) in *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing* 15(4), pp.27-43 (later editions including pp.27-75). *Source: Godfrey introduction; Haigh 2014 footnote 2.*
- **1996**: Edwards, *The Closed World: Computers and the Politics of Discourse in Cold War America* (MIT Press) — situates the EDVAC and its successors in their Cold War funding context. *(Cited but not directly anchored for Ch8.)*
- **December 2012**: Mai Sugimoto reveals that the APS Library copy of the *First Draft* contains additional sections (§§13.6-13.7 and a final-table description of three planned EDVAC models) not present in the Moore School copy. *Source: M. Sugimoto, "On the original copies of 'First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC' by John von Neumann," PHS Studies No.6, Feb 2012, pp.83-89; cited in Godfrey's 2020 update note.*
- **January-March 2014**: Haigh, Priestley, and Rope publish "Reconsidering the Stored-Program Concept" in *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing* 36(1), pp.4-17 — the historiographic reframing this chapter relies on for the three-paradigm decomposition and the 1949-coinage finding. *Source: verified by Claude 2026-04-28.*
