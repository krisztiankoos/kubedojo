# People: Chapter 8 — The Stored Program

Each entry lists name, dates (where verified), role in this chapter, and the specific anchor in `sources.md` that grounds the role-in-this-chapter claim. Entries marked **Yellow** lack a primary anchor Claude verified on 2026-04-28.

## Engineers and architects

- **John von Neumann (1903-1957)** — Mathematician at the Institute for Advanced Study; consultant to the Moore School EDVAC project from summer 1944. Sole signatory of the *First Draft* (June 30, 1945); co-author of the IAS *Preliminary Discussion* (28 June 1946). The chapter's stance: he was the document's writer-up and the credentialed signal-amplifier, not the sole author of the ideas it organized. **Anchor**: First Draft title page (Green); Burks/Goldstine/von Neumann 1946 preface (Green); Eckert OH 13 pp.46-47 for one party's interpretive reading (Yellow as motive).

- **J. Presper Eckert Jr. (1919-1995)** — Chief electrical engineer of the ENIAC project at the Moore School. Designed the ENIAC's circuitry; co-developer with Mauchly of the EDVAC successor concept; resigned from the Moore School in March 1946 over patent assignment. Gave the OH 13 oral history to Nancy Stern in October 1977 — the chapter's primary source for the credit-dispute reading. **Anchor**: Eckert OH 13 (Green for the transcript itself; Yellow for the dating of the resignation).

- **John Mauchly (1907-1980)** — Physics professor at Ursinus College who proposed the ENIAC concept in his August 1942 Moore School memorandum (Yellow for that document; standard reference). Co-developer of the EDVAC concept with Eckert; resigned from the Moore School with Eckert in March 1946. Mentioned exactly once in the *First Draft* body — a multiplication encoding scheme attributed to "J. W. Mauchly" — and not on the title page. **Anchor**: First Draft body grep (Green for the single-mention); August 1942 memorandum (Yellow).

- **Herman H. Goldstine (1913-2004)** — Army Captain (later Lt. Col.); the U.S. Army Ordnance liaison to the Moore School ENIAC project. Distributed the *First Draft* by mailing copies in June 1945. Co-author of the IAS *Preliminary Discussion* (1946). Wrote *The Computer from Pascal to von Neumann* (1972) — the standard insider history. The chapter's stance: he is the connector who turned a Moore School document into a worldwide artifact. **Anchor**: Burks/Goldstine/von Neumann 1946 preface (Green); Light 1999 p.469 for his selection of the six women programmers (Green); Goldstine 1972 (Yellow — physical book).

- **Arthur W. Burks (1915-2008)** — Mathematician at the Moore School; later moved to the IAS in March 1946 and to the University of Michigan by September 1946. First-named co-author of the IAS *Preliminary Discussion* (28 June 1946). Notable in the chapter as part of the named-author IAS report that the *First Draft* notably wasn't. **Anchor**: Burks/Goldstine/von Neumann 1946 title page (Green); Eckert OH 13 transcript (mentions Burks's 1946 transition to IAS, then Michigan; Green).

## The ENIAC programmers (the six women)

All six women selected by Goldstine in 1945 to program ENIAC. **Light 1999 p.469** is the single source that names the six together; **Bartik CHM oral history pp.21-22** corroborates the list and adds personal detail. Both anchors are Green. The personal details below are drawn from Light 1999 p.467 footnote and the Bartik CHM oral history; later married names are well-attested across multiple secondary sources.

- **Kathleen "Kay" McNulty (1921-2006)** — Mathematics graduate (1942) of Chestnut Hill College for Women; hired as a human computer at the Moore School. Selected in 1945 as one of the six original ENIAC programmers. Married John Mauchly after the war (his second wife). The McNulty quote at Light 1999 p.470 — "Somebody gave us a whole stack of blueprints, and these were the wiring diagrams for all the panels, and they said 'Here, figure out how the machine works and then figure out how to program it'" — is one of the chapter's load-bearing primary quotations.
- **Frances Bilas (later Spence) (1922-2012)** — Mathematics graduate of Chestnut Hill; selected as one of the six original ENIAC programmers. Photographed setting program switches on the ENIAC during the February 1946 public unveiling.
- **Betty Jean Jennings Bartik (1924-2011)** — Mathematics graduate of Northwest Missouri State Teachers College; the chapter's narrator-voice for the post-1945 ENIAC retrofit (via the 2008 CHM oral history). Married a Moore School engineer; died 2011. **Anchor**: Bartik CHM oral history (Green for everything she narrates).
- **Ruth Lichterman (later Teitelbaum) (1924-1986)** — Mathematics graduate of Hunter College; one of the six original ENIAC programmers; later moved to Aberdeen with the ENIAC.
- **Elizabeth Snyder (later Holberton) (1917-2001)** — Journalism graduate of Penn; one of the six original ENIAC programmers. Co-developed the demonstration trajectory program with Betty Jean Jennings (Light 1999 p.470). Married John Holberton, the Army Ordnance Department supervisor who oversaw the programming team.
- **Marlyn Wescoff (later Meltzer) (1922-2008)** — Mathematics graduate of Temple University; one of the six original ENIAC programmers. Worked on the ENIAC project from 1942 (as a human computer) through the programming retrofit.

## Supervisors and supporting figures

- **John Holberton** *(Yellow)* — Army Ordnance Department civilian, supervisor of the six women ENIAC operators; later married Elizabeth Snyder. **Anchor**: Light 1999 p.469 (Green for his role).

- **Adele Goldstine (1920-1964)** — Mathematician; wife of Herman Goldstine; trained the first ENIAC programmers in 1945 and wrote the technical manual *Report on the ENIAC* (1946). Joined the 1948 ENIAC stored-program retrofit team and made consulting trips to Princeton with Bartik. **Anchor**: Bartik CHM oral history p.32 (Green for the 1948 retrofit role); Light 1999 p.469 (Green for the training role).

- **Richard Clippinger (1913-1997)** — Ballistics Research Laboratory mathematician; in 1948 he wrote a contract with the Moore School to convert ENIAC to read instructions from its function tables. Bartik's account: he "had thought of a 3-address instruction. But, anyway, he talked to von Neumann, and von Neumann said no, to use a 1-address instruction." **Anchor**: Bartik CHM oral history pp.31-32 (Green).

- **John G. Brainerd (1904-1988)** *(Yellow on character framing; Green on role)* — First director of the ENIAC project at the Moore School. Eckert's 1977 OH 13 testimony is sharply hostile to Brainerd (Eckert OH 13 around p.47-49 line 1018 onward). The chapter must record Eckert's framing as one party's reading, not as established fact about Brainerd's actual role. **Anchor**: Eckert OH 13 (Green for the existence of the testimony; Yellow for any character claim).

- **Nathaniel Rochester (1919-2001)** — IBM engineer at Poughkeepsie; led the team that built IBM's first electronic computer ("Test Assembly," based on the 604 Electronic Calculating Punch); author of the May 1949 internal report titled "A Calculator Using Electrostatic Storage and a Stored Program" — the earliest located use of the phrase "stored program" in a written document. Moves the term forward into the IBM 701 product. **Anchor**: Haigh 2014 p.5 (Green — Haigh-Priestley-Rope's archival finding); Haigh 2014 footnote 16 (IBM Corporate Archives, Somers NY).

- **Maurice Wilkes (1913-2010)** *(supporting; not chapter-internal)* — Cambridge mathematician; head of the team that built EDSAC (first successful run, May 1949); attended the 1946 Moore School Lectures. **Anchor**: Haigh 2014 p.5 (Green for his attendance at the Moore School Lectures and EDSAC's May 1949 first run).

- **F.C. Williams and Tom Kilburn (Manchester)** *(supporting; not chapter-internal)* — Built the Manchester Baby that ran the first stored program on June 21, 1948. **Anchor**: Haigh 2014 p.7 (Green for the Manchester Baby date).

## Historiographers and witnesses

- **Thomas Haigh (b. 1972)** — Associate professor of information studies at the University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee; PhD in history and sociology of science from the University of Pennsylvania. Lead author of "Reconsidering the Stored-Program Concept" (2014) and the broader ENIAC In Action research project. The chapter's primary historiographic frame is Haigh's three-paradigm decomposition.

- **Mark Priestley** — Independent researcher with a PhD from University College London; author of *A Science of Operations* (Springer, 2011). Co-author of Haigh-Priestley-Rope 2014.

- **Crispin Rope** — Avocational ENIAC researcher with prior publications in *IEEE Annals* and *Resurrection: The Bulletin of the Computer Conservation Society*. Co-author of Haigh-Priestley-Rope 2014.

- **Jennifer S. Light (b. ~1970)** — Historian of science (PhD Harvard); author of "When Computers Were Women" (1999, *Technology and Culture*). The chapter's primary frame for the gendered division of programming labor at the Moore School.

- **Nancy B. Stern** — Conducted the Eckert OH 13 oral history in October 1977; later wrote *From ENIAC to UNIVAC* (1981). Both the OH 13 transcript and her 1981 book are framed by her stated goal of correcting what she saw as the 1973 Honeywell-Sperry ruling's underestimation of Mauchly. **Anchor**: Eckert OH 13 (Green for the transcript itself); Stern 1981 (Yellow — physical book).

- **Michael D. Godfrey** — Stanford Information Systems Lab; in 1992 produced the TeX reset of the *First Draft* from the Moore School and APS Library typescripts that the chapter relies on for primary-text anchors. Co-author with David Hendry of "The Computer as von Neumann Planned It" (1993, *IEEE Annals* 15(1)). **Anchor**: Godfrey introduction in edvac-IA.txt (Green for his role as editor of the modern reset).

## Notable absences

- **Alan Turing** is mentioned in the *First Draft*'s afterlife (Turing cited the *First Draft* in his 1945 *Proposals for the Pilot ACE*, per Godfrey 1992 introduction footnote 1) but the chapter does not bring Turing into scene. Ch10 (the Imitation Game) is where Turing belongs in this book's structure.
- **John Atanasoff** appears only as a name in the 1973 Honeywell-Sperry ruling (one of the four grounds for invalidating the ENIAC patent was that Eckert and Mauchly's work derived from Atanasoff's earlier ABC computer). The chapter must mention this in the 1973 scene but not retell the Atanasoff-Berry priority dispute, which belongs to Ch7's already-drafted analog/digital territory.
- **Norbert Wiener** is not in scope. Cybernetics is Ch6's territory.
- **Newell, Simon, McCarthy, Minsky, Shannon** are not in scope. They appear in Ch10-Ch13.
