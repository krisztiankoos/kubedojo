# People: Chapter 32 — The DARPA SUR Program

Verified protagonist bios. Bios are kept narrowly to the 1966-1988 ASR institutional arc the chapter covers. Verification colors track how confident the chapter can be in load-bearing biographical claims when drafting.

## The Newell Study Group (May 1971)

### Allen Newell (1927-1992)
- **Position in 1971:** University Professor at Carnegie-Mellon University; co-architect (with Herbert Simon) of the Logic Theorist and the General Problem Solver.
- **Role in the chapter:** Chairman of the ARPA-IPTO study group whose May 1971 report became the SUR program's institutional charter. Authored or co-authored Conclusions 1-7 (Section 1.1) and the methodological proposal in Section 8.6 ("Public Data and Public Analysis").
- **Why he matters here:** Newell is the chapter's authorship figure for the institutional choreography that outlived SUR. The methodological proposal in §8.6 — common task descriptions, instrumented systems, publicly available data — is the through-line that connects 1971 to Charles Wayne's 1980s revival.
- **Verification:** **Green** — chairmanship verified via the Newell 1971 cover page (PDF p.3); Newell's broader CMU position verified via standard secondary sources. Bios on Newell-the-Logic-Theorist-author belong to Ch12, not Ch32.

### Dennis H. Klatt (1938-1988)
- **Position in 1971:** MIT Research Laboratory of Electronics; speech-perception and speech-synthesis researcher.
- **Role in the chapter:** Member of the Newell 1971 study group (cover page). Author of the December 1977 *JASA* "Review of the ARPA Speech Understanding Project" — the canonical retrospective on the September 1976 demonstration.
- **Why he matters here:** Klatt is the most-cited single secondary source on the SUR program's outcomes. As both a study-group co-author (1971) and the program's reviewer (1977), his writing bridges the charter and the demonstration. The chapter cites Klatt 1977 as a citation pointer; the Klatt PDF is paywalled and not currently in hand for page-anchored claims.
- **Verification:** **Green** for study-group membership (Newell 1971 cover) and for the 1977 *JASA* citation; **Yellow** for any specific page-level claim from Klatt 1977.

### J. C. R. Licklider (1915-1990)
- **Position in 1971:** ARPA-IPTO institutional figure; previously the founding director of IPTO (1962-1964) and a key architect of ARPA's computing-research portfolio.
- **Role in the chapter:** Member of the Newell 1971 study group. His presence on the study group binds the SUR charter to the broader IPTO institutional culture.
- **Why he matters here:** Licklider's role anchors the chapter's claim that SUR's methodological choreography came from the broader ARPA-IPTO institutional culture, not from speech-recognition researchers in isolation.
- **Verification:** **Green** for study-group membership (Newell 1971 cover); broader bio is well-documented in standard secondary sources. Licklider's "Man-Computer Symbiosis" / IPTO-founding work belongs to earlier chapters.

### Raj Reddy (b. 1937)
- **Position in 1971:** Faculty member at Carnegie-Mellon University; lead investigator of CMU's speech-recognition group.
- **Role in the chapter:** Member of the Newell 1971 study group. Principal investigator of CMU's SUR contracts. Co-author with Bruce Lowerre of the 1979 chapter that documents HARPY's targets-vs-performance.
- **Why he matters here:** Reddy is the institutional bridge between the Newell 1971 charter and the CMU contractor systems (Hearsay-I, Dragon, Hearsay-II, HARPY) that delivered against it.
- **Verification:** **Green** for study-group membership (Newell 1971 cover); **Green** for the institutional role at CMU (Lowerre & Reddy 1979 chapter authorship; CMU 1977 SUR Summary Report).

### Other study group members (J. Barnett, J. Forgie, C. Green, J. Munson, W. Woods)
- **Roles:** Co-authors of the 1971 report. Their specific institutional affiliations and within-study contributions are not load-bearing for the chapter's narrative; Newell (chair), Klatt (later reviewer), Licklider (IPTO), and Reddy (CMU PI) are the four whose bios shape the 1971-1977 arc.
- **Verification:** **Green** for study-group membership via the Newell 1971 cover. Specific within-report attributions to individual co-authors are not pursued in this contract.

## The 1969 Critic

### John R. Pierce (1910-2002)
- **Position in 1969:** Bell Telephone Laboratories, Murray Hill NJ; influential engineer, executive, and writer; coined the term "transistor"; oversaw the development of Echo I (the first communications satellite). Previously chaired the 1966 Automatic Language Processing Advisory Committee (ALPAC) for the National Academy of Sciences.
- **Role in the chapter:** Author of the 1969 *JASA* letter "Whither Speech Recognition?" — the critique that defined the funding climate the Newell 1971 study group was responding to. The chapter's Scene 1 builds from Pierce's letter to the Newell charter.
- **Why he matters here:** Pierce is the chapter's antagonist-of-record for the 1966-1971 funding climate, but he is *not* a SUR antagonist — by 1971, when the Newell study group convened, ALPAC's machine-translation defunding was already five years old. The chapter must avoid implying Pierce was reacting to SUR (he was reacting to a prior generation of speech-recognition claims).
- **Verification:** **Green** for the 1966 ALPAC chairmanship and the 1969 JASA letter citation (Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.3 plus Church 2018 NLE both confirm). Pierce's broader Bell Labs career is well-documented; not load-bearing for Ch32 beyond the institutional-weight framing.

## The IBM Parallel Track

### Frederick Jelinek (1932-2010)
- **Position in 1971+:** Manager of the Continuous Speech Recognition Group at the IBM Thomas J. Watson Research Center, Yorktown Heights NY (joined 1972; led the group through the 1970s and early 1980s). Previously Cornell University faculty and an information theorist by training (Czech-born, M.I.T. Ph.D. 1962).
- **Role in the chapter:** Lead author of the IBM-statistical-ASR program that ran in parallel to (not within) the DARPA SUR contractor effort. Co-author of the 1983 PAMI paper. Author of the December 1988 talk that introduced the "fire a linguist" quote into circulation. Author of the 2005 LREC retrospective that documents the quote's provenance.
- **Why he matters here:** Jelinek is the chapter's protagonist of the parallel statistical-ASR track that, twelve years after SUR ended, would inherit the SUR-era measurement methodology and apply it to win the late-1980s/1990s common-task evaluations.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the IBM Yorktown Heights affiliation (Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 cover page); **Green** for the 1988 quote provenance (Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.2); **Green** for the 2005 LREC invited-talk role (PDF cover and slide footers).

### Lalit R. Bahl (n.d.)
- **Position in 1971+:** IBM T. J. Watson Research Center; member of the Continuous Speech Recognition Group.
- **Role in the chapter:** First-listed author of the 1983 PAMI paper "A Maximum Likelihood Approach to Continuous Speech Recognition." Co-architect of the IBM-statistical approach with Jelinek and Mercer.
- **Why he matters here:** Bahl is the technical co-author of the chapter's Scene 4 anchor source. Specific within-IBM contributions are not pursued in this contract.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the 1983 PAMI co-authorship (paper's bio note: "B.Tech.(Hons.) from the Indian Institute of Technology, Kharagpur, India, in 1964 and the M.S. and Ph.D. degrees in electrical engineering from the University of Illinois, Urbana"). Broader bio not load-bearing.

### Robert L. Mercer (b. 1946)
- **Position in 1971+:** IBM T. J. Watson Research Center; member of the Continuous Speech Recognition Group.
- **Role in the chapter:** Third-listed author of the 1983 PAMI paper. The "There is no data like more data" maxim that Jelinek attributes to Mercer at Arden House 1985 is a useful sublegend.
- **Why he matters here:** Mercer's "no data like more data" maxim captures the IBM group's emerging methodological stance — and is a small but useful anchor for the chapter's Scene 5 transition into the post-SUR era. Mercer's later career (statistical machine translation; Renaissance Technologies) is out of scope.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the 1983 PAMI co-authorship; **Green** for the Arden House 1985 attribution via Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.5.

## The Within-SUR Statistical Exception

### James K. Baker (b. 1947)
- **Position in 1971-1975:** Carnegie-Mellon University Ph.D. student under Raj Reddy; built the Dragon system as an early statistical-method speech-recognition system within CMU's SUR contract.
- **Role in the chapter:** The single statistical-methods exception within the SUR contractor portfolio. Dragon's design fed forward into HARPY (which adopted Dragon's integrated network representation but dropped its a-priori transition probabilities). Baker is mentioned by name in Jelinek 2005's "Situation in 1970s" slide as the exception that proves the rule of SUR's AI-method dominance.
- **Why he matters here:** Baker prevents the chapter's narrative from collapsing into a clean "SUR was AI; IBM was statistics" dichotomy. Within SUR, statistics had a place — it was just not the dominant approach.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the Dragon-as-SUR-statistical-exception framing — verified via Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.3 and CMU 1977 SUR Summary Report Section "The Dragon System" (PDF p.11). Baker's later founding of Dragon Systems (1982) is confirmed in Juang & Rabiner 2005 PDF p.9 ("Jim Baker, who moved to Massachusetts to start a company with the same name in the early 1980s") but is out of scope for Ch32.

## The Demonstration Authors

### Bruce T. Lowerre (n.d.)
- **Position in 1976:** Carnegie-Mellon University Ph.D. student under Raj Reddy; principal architect of the HARPY system.
- **Role in the chapter:** Author of the April 1976 HARPY thesis; co-author with Reddy of the 1979 *Trends in Speech Recognition* chapter that documents HARPY's targets-vs-performance.
- **Why he matters here:** Lowerre is the principal author of the SUR program's headline result — the 1976 HARPY demonstration that met the Newell 1971 Final Specifications.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the thesis date and HARPY authorship (Stanford-hosted Thesis Summary cover; Lowerre & Reddy 1979 chapter authorship). Specific later-career bio not load-bearing for Ch32.

## The Methodological Heir

### Charles L. Wayne (n.d.)
- **Position in mid-1980s:** DARPA program manager who restarted US speech-and-language research funding through the institutionalization of the "common task method."
- **Role in the chapter:** The institutional figure who, in the mid-1980s, revived DARPA speech funding by structurally returning to Newell 1971 §8.6's methodological proposal — well-defined objective evaluation, neutral evaluator (NIST), shared datasets — applied this time to statistical methods.
- **Why he matters here:** Wayne is the chapter's narrative-payoff figure. The Newell 1971 methodological proposal that the SUR contractor systems built against in 1971-1976 is the same proposal that Wayne re-institutionalized in the mid-1980s for the next generation of speech research. The chapter's claim is that this is the SUR program's most durable contribution.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the mid-1980s DARPA revival role and the "common task method" framing — verified via Church 2018 NLE tribute to Charles Wayne.

## Notes on bios

- All bios are kept narrowly to the 1966-1988 ASR institutional arc that the chapter covers. Earlier work (Pierce coining "transistor"; Newell co-authoring Logic Theorist; Licklider founding IPTO) and later work (Mercer at Renaissance Technologies; Baker founding Dragon Systems; Sphinx and the 1990s benchmark culture) are deliberately out of scope.
- No invented quotes or attributed motives. Where a bio implies a stance (e.g., "Pierce's letter shaped the funding climate"), the implication must be cited from a primary or convergent secondary source — never invented.
- The chapter's authorship figure is Newell (charter) but its protagonist is Jelinek (parallel track + the December 1988 quote that gives the chapter its hook). The chapter's antagonist is *not* Pierce — it is the rule-based AI methodology that Newell's own charter institutionalized and that HARPY rode to success in 1976.
- Where bios cite specific dates that are not anchored at page level (e.g., Mercer's birth year), the dates are background context and not load-bearing for any claim in the chapter's Scene-Level Claim Table.
