# Brief: Chapter 32 — The DARPA SUR Program

## Thesis

The DARPA Speech Understanding Research program (1971-1976) is more often remembered for the contractor systems it produced — HARPY, HEARSAY-II, BBN HWIM, SDC — than for the methodological revolution buried in Section 8.6 of Allen Newell's 1971 study-group report. SUR's enduring contribution was not the demonstration that connected speech recognition could meet a 1,000-word target; it was the institutional choreography that made such a target *legible* in the first place: a written charter with quantified specifications, common task descriptions, instrumented systems, and publicly available high-quality data on which competing contractors could be objectively compared. The chapter argues that what Frederick Jelinek's IBM team and, later, Charles Wayne's mid-1980s DARPA revival did was inherit and extend Newell 1971's measurement methodology — applying it to statistical models that had no place in the original SUR contractor portfolio. The "fire a linguist" quote, dated December 1988 and well after SUR had ended, was the *consequence* of that inherited methodology, not its cause. Whether SUR itself was an AI-research success (HARPY did meet the Final Specifications) or an institutional disappointment (DARPA cut speech funding for roughly a decade afterward) is a question the chapter leaves layered, not collapsed.

## Scope

- IN SCOPE: The 1969 Pierce *JASA* letter and the 1966 ALPAC report as the funding climate the SUR study group was responding to; the May 1971 Newell et al. *Speech-Understanding Systems: Final Report of a Study Group* — its Conclusions, its two-column Initial vs. Final Specifications table (Section 1.2), and its "Public Data and Public Analysis" methodological proposal (Section 8.6); the September 1976 four-system demonstration (HARPY, HEARSAY-II, BBN HWIM, SDC) and the CMU 1977 / Lowerre-Reddy 1979 reporting on the targets-vs-achievement table; IBM's parallel maximum-likelihood / Markov-source-modeling effort under Bahl, Jelinek, and Mercer (1983 PAMI paper); the verifiable December 1988 / Wayne-PA provenance of Jelinek's "fire a linguist" quote; the post-1976 ASR funding winter and the mid-1980s Charles Wayne revival via the "common task method" (Church 2018 NLE).
- OUT OF SCOPE: Deep mathematical derivation of Hidden Markov Models (covered in Part 5); Sphinx and the late-1980s/1990s benchmark culture beyond a forward-pointer (covered in later chapters); the 2010s deep-learning ASR revolution (covered in Part 7); machine-translation history beyond the ALPAC connection (covered separately); biographical detail on Pierce, Newell, Jelinek, or Reddy beyond their roles in the 1969-1988 ASR institutional arc.

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not turn the DARPA SUR program into a morality play of statistics defeating linguistics. SUR's institutional setup demanded measurable performance on a fixed task — and HARPY *met* that target through a rule-and-search hybrid (a finite-state network compiled from lexical, phonological, and syntactic rules) that was not statistical in the Markov-source sense Bahl-Jelinek-Mercer would later use. The "AI-vs-statistics" frame is a useful aftershock narrative, not a 1971-1976 reality. Within SUR itself, the only statistical-method exception was Jim Baker's CMU Dragon system, which fed into HARPY's design rather than competing with it.

The chapter must also not retroject Jelinek's December 1988 "fire a linguist" quote into the 1971-1976 SUR period. The quote is, in Jelinek's own 2005 retrospective, a December 1988 talk at the Workshop on Evaluation of NLP Systems, Wayne PA — twelve years after SUR ended. Using it as in-period commentary is a chronological error.

The chapter must not invent dialogue or institutional motives. Every quoted sentence must come from a primary or transmitted-verbatim secondary source (Newell 1971 report; Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983; Jelinek 2005 LREC slides; Church 2018 NLE; CMU 1977 SUR Summary Report; Lowerre & Reddy 1979 chapter). DARPA program-management decisions to cut speech funding after 1976 are documented obliquely in secondary sources (Church 2018, Juang & Rabiner 2005) but not via internal ARPA-IPTO records — frame them in prose with appropriate hedging ("according to subsequent retrospectives") rather than as direct fact.

The chapter must not claim SUR was a "failure" or a "success" without scoping the verdict. By the criterion Newell 1971 itself used — at least one contractor system meets the Final Specifications by 1976 — SUR succeeded (HARPY did). By the post-hoc criterion of "did DARPA continue funding speech-understanding research after 1976" — SUR was followed by a roughly decade-long funding pause that Church 2018 calls a "funding winter." Both verdicts are real and the chapter should hold them in tension.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Letter and the Climate (1966-1969).** John Pierce's 1969 *JASA* letter "Whither Speech Recognition?" against the backdrop of his 1966 ALPAC chairmanship and the prior collapse of US machine-translation funding. Why ARPA-IPTO needed an unusually rigorous study-group exercise before committing fresh ASR money.
2. **The Newell Charter (May 1971).** The Newell-chaired study group's report. Its Conclusions (three years insufficient, five years reasonable). Its two-column Initial vs. Final Specifications table — what the program would and would not promise. Its methodological insistence on public data, common task descriptions, and instrumented evaluation (Section 8.6).
3. **The September 1976 Demonstration.** HARPY meeting the Final Specifications at 1,011 words, 5% semantic error, 80× real-time, operational on 13 August 1976. HEARSAY-II achieving similar accuracy at 2-20× slower. BBN HWIM and SDC not meeting the targets. The methodological choreography — same task, same evaluator, comparable numbers — working as Newell 1971 §8.6 had described.
4. **The IBM Parallel Track (early 1970s through 1983).** Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer at IBM Yorktown Heights formulating speech recognition as maximum-likelihood decoding over a noisy channel. Markov-source models. The Forward-Backward Algorithm for parameter estimation. Perplexity as a task-difficulty measure that displaced "vocabulary size" as the headline metric. Running experiments on CMU's AIX05 task — the same benchmark CMU used for HARPY — to compare statistical decoding against the SUR contractor results.
5. **The Quote and the Methodology That Outlived SUR.** Jelinek's December 1988 Wayne-PA talk where the "fire a linguist" quote was first uttered, and his 2005 LREC retrospective documenting it. The post-1976 ASR funding winter (Church 2018: roughly 1975-1986 in US ASR/MT). Charles Wayne's mid-1980s DARPA revival via the "common task method" — structurally a return to Newell 1971 §8.6, but applied to the statistical methods that won. Honest close: SUR's contractor systems are mostly forgotten; SUR's measurement methodology underwrites every benchmark culture that followed.

## Conflict Notes

- **The "fire a linguist" quote is widely circulated in mutated forms** ("Every time I fire a linguist…", "Whenever I fire a linguist…", "Anytime I fire a linguist…"). Jelinek's own 2005 LREC retrospective gives the verifiable December 1988 / Wayne PA provenance and the wording "Whenever I fire a linguist our system performance improves." Use Jelinek's wording.
- **HARPY exceeded vs. met the Newell 1971 specifications** — both phrasings are anchored. Lowerre & Reddy 1979 says "Harpy not only met all the specifications but exceeded several of the stated objectives"; the CMU 1977 report uses the more measured "It not only satisfies the original goals, but exceeds some of the stated objectives." Prefer the CMU 1977 phrasing in prose.
- **Was SUR a "success" or a "failure"?** — Two equally well-anchored verdicts apply at different time scales. The chapter should hold both: HARPY met the 1976 Final Specifications by Newell 1971's own criterion; DARPA cut speech funding for roughly a decade after 1976 by Church 2018's criterion.
- **Did DARPA "expect" SUR to fail?** — This claim circulates in popular ASR histories and is not supported by the Newell 1971 report itself, which explicitly forecast a "moderate chance of success" for the Final Specifications in 1976 (Section 1.2). Prose should not endorse the "DARPA-knew-it-would-fail" reading without primary anchoring.
- **Was IBM part of SUR?** — No. IBM ran its Continuous Speech Recognition Group at Yorktown Heights independently. Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 explicitly notes the IBM team ran experiments on the same CMU-AIX05 task SUR used (Section X, p.189), confirming the parallel-track framing. The chapter must not imply IBM was a SUR contractor.
- **The Pierce 1969 letter as a load-bearing primary source** — the JASA PDF is paywalled. Verbatim text in this contract is anchored via Jelinek's 2005 LREC slides quoting Pierce, plus Church 2018 NLE quoting Pierce, with two-source convergence on identical wording. Treat this as a Green-by-convergence anchor for the *quoted text*, but flag the missing direct PDF in sources.md and open-questions.md.
