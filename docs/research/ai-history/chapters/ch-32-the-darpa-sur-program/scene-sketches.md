## Scene 1 — The Letter and the Climate (1966-1969)

Anchored layer: *600-900 words: The 1966-1969 funding climate* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| In 1966, John Pierce — by then a senior figure at Bell Telephone Laboratories — chaired the Automatic Language Processing Advisory Committee (ALPAC) for the National Academy of Sciences. The committee's report effectively ended US government funding for machine-translation research for over a decade. | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.3 ("J.R. Pierce Activities" slide); Church 2018 NLE tribute to Charles Wayne (1966 ALPAC date and impact). | **Green** |
| In October 1969, Pierce published "Whither Speech Recognition?" as a Letter to the Editor in *The Journal of the Acoustical Society of America* 46(4B): 1049-1051. | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.2 (Pierce-quote slide cites the JASA letter); Church 2018 NLE (1969 publication date). | **Green** for the bibliographic citation. The original PDF is paywalled at AIP. |
| Pierce wrote that automatic-speech-recognition research was "attractive to money. The attraction is perhaps similar to the attraction of schemes for turning water into gasoline, extracting gold from the sea, or going to the moon." | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.2 (verbatim transmission within quotation marks). | **Green** by convergence (Jelinek slide quotes Pierce verbatim; Church 2018 confirms the substance). |
| Pierce also wrote that "Most recognizers behave not like scientists, but like mad inventors or untrustworthy engineers", and that ASR performance "will continue to be very limited unless the recognizing device understands what is being said with something of the facility of a native speaker (that is, better than a foreigner fluent in the language)." | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.2 (verbatim transmission). | **Green** by convergence with Church 2018. |
| Pierce closed his letter with a line directly applicable to AI as a whole: "Any application of the foregoing discussion to work in the general area of pattern recognition is left as an exercise for the reader." | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.2-3 (verbatim transmission across two slides). | **Green** by convergence. |
| The 1969 letter and the 1966 ALPAC report together created a 1970-1971 funding climate in which any new ARPA-IPTO commitment to speech-understanding research had to be unusually rigorously argued. | Newell 1971 cover acknowledgment to ARPA-IPTO (PDF p.3); Newell 1971 Section 1.1 introduction ("We were charged with determining the feasibility of demonstrating a speech recognition system with useful capabilities and greater power than current isolated word recognition programs", PDF p.11) — the language of the charge itself reflects the post-Pierce climate. | **Green** for the framing — the inferential bridge from Pierce's critique to the Newell study group is widely supported and the Newell report's defensive opening confirms the framing. |

**Scene-level note:** The scene's narrative job is to establish the *funding climate* the Newell study group was responding to — without overclaiming that Pierce or ALPAC *caused* the SUR program. The causal chain is: ALPAC (1966) → MT defunding → general skepticism toward AI-adjacent natural-language research → Pierce 1969 reinforces the skepticism for ASR specifically → ARPA-IPTO commissions the Newell study group in 1970-1971 to make a defensible case for speech-understanding funding. Avoid prose that paints Pierce as either a villain (he was making a substantive technical argument) or as the SUR program's direct adversary (by 1971 the institutional question had moved on).

## Scene 2 — The Newell Charter (May 1971)

Anchored layer: *1,100-1,500 words: The Newell 1971 charter as institutional artifact* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| In May 1971, the ARPA-IPTO-commissioned study group — chaired by Allen Newell, with members J. Barnett, J. Forgie, C. Green, D. Klatt, J. C. R. Licklider, J. Munson, R. Reddy, and W. Woods — published *Speech-Understanding Systems: Final Report of a Study Group* as a Carnegie-Mellon University Computer Science Department technical report. | Newell 1971 cover (PDF p.3). | **Green** — verified by `pdftotext` extraction 2026-04-28. |
| The report explicitly framed its charge: "We were charged with determining the feasibility of demonstrating a speech recognition system with useful capabilities and greater power than current isolated word recognition programs (e.g., Vicens, Gold)." | Newell 1971 Section 1.1 (PDF p.11). | **Green** — verbatim. |
| Conclusion 1 of the report: "Three years is not enough time to achieve a system with the initial specifications." | Newell 1971 Section 1.1 (PDF p.11). | **Green** — verbatim. |
| Conclusion 2 of the report: "Five years provides a reasonable chance of success for the system with the final specifications. The system would be a research prototype, though it would be capable of extensive operation for exploration and testing." | Newell 1971 Section 1.1 (PDF p.11). | **Green** — verbatim. |
| Section 1.2 set out two side-by-side specifications. The Initial Specifications were the more ambitious set: 10,000 words, telephone-quality audio, demonstrable in 1973. The Final Specifications were what the program would actually commit to: 1,000 words, good-quality microphone, demonstrable in 1976. | Newell 1971 Section 1.2 Figure 1.1 (PDF p.12). | **Green** — verified by `pdftotext -layout` extraction. |
| The Final Specifications committed contractors to: continuous speech (1); from many cooperative speakers of the General American dialect (2-3); in a quiet room (4) with a good-quality microphone (5); slight per-speaker tuning (6); a slightly selected vocabulary of 1,000 words (8); a highly artificial syntax (9); a task like data management or computer status — but not the computer-consultant task (10); a simple psychological model of the user (11); graceful interaction (12); tolerating less than 10% semantic error (13); in a few times real time (14); demonstrable in 1976 with a moderate chance of success (19). | Newell 1971 Section 1.2 Figure 1.1 (PDF p.12), items (1)-(19) of the Final Specifications column. | **Green** for the entire spec list — verified verbatim. |
| Conclusion 5 of the report: "Success requires widespread involvement by several technical communities (principally from within the computer, speech and communication sciences). In particular, especially for the research and early development aspects, effort and attention must be focussed on the ultimate problem of a speech-understanding system through some form of cooperative and evaluative endeavor." | Newell 1971 Section 1.1 (PDF p.11). | **Green** — verbatim. |
| Section 8.6 *Public Data and Public Analysis* — the methodological proposal that becomes the chapter's central infrastructure claim — argues: "A major instrument for progress on speech-understanding systems will be good data of suitable variety, prepared so that it is possible to relate how different systems and algorithms process it. Claims will be made about a wide variety of systems and subsystems over a wide variety of communication situations. If the claims are not made against a background of publicly available high quality data of known structure, it will never be possible to understand the claims or their basis. The issue is not one primarily of assigning credit, but of making progress by understanding success and failure." | Newell 1971 Section 8.6 (PDF p.50). | **Green** — verbatim. This is the chapter's load-bearing methodological anchor. |
| Section 8.7 extended the methodology in three directions: adequate task description, instrumenting systems for measurement, and operating-total-system as a contractor-readiness criterion. | Newell 1971 Section 8.7 (PDF p.50-51). | **Green** — verified. |
| The report did not propose a unified single-contractor program. Conclusion 5's "cooperative and evaluative endeavor" framing institutionalized a multi-contractor design from the outset — different sites racing toward the same Final Specifications, with cross-site comparison as the success criterion. | Newell 1971 Section 1.1 Conclusion 5 (PDF p.11) plus Section 8.6 (PDF p.50). | **Green** for the institutional framing. |

**Scene-level note:** This is the chapter's deepest-anchored scene and should not be padded. The narrative move is to read the Newell 1971 report as an *institutional artifact* — what it asks for, what it explicitly does not promise, and what methodological choreography it institutionalizes. Avoid prose that explains what speech understanding is, what continuous speech recognition entails, or what HMMs are; those belong to other chapters. This scene's job is the charter as charter.

## Scene 3 — The September 1976 Demonstration

Anchored layer: *800-1,100 words: The September 1976 demonstration* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| The HARPY system at Carnegie-Mellon University became operationally configured for the 1,011-word document-retrieval task on 13 August 1976 — six weeks before the September 1976 ARPA demonstration. | Lowerre & Reddy 1979 Figure 15-1: "Operational 13 August 1976." | **Green** — verified by `pdftotext` extraction. |
| HARPY was demonstrated against the Newell 1971 Final Specifications and met or exceeded each specification line. The CMU 1977 retrospective frames this measuredly: "It not only satisfies the original goals, but exceeds some of the stated objectives." | CMU 1977 Section I (PDF p.7). | **Green** — verbatim. Use this phrasing rather than the more enthusiastic Lowerre-Reddy 1979 "exceeded several." |
| Specifically, HARPY recognized speech from a 1,011-word vocabulary at 5% semantic error against the Final Specifications' "less than 10% semantic error" target. | CMU 1977 Figure 1 (PDF p.8); Lowerre & Reddy 1979 Figure 15-1. | **Green** — verified by both CMU primary anchors. |
| HARPY ran 80× real-time on a .35 MIPS PDP-KA10 (28 MIPSS), where the Newell 1971 Final Specification's "few times real time" target had been benchmarked against an assumed 100 MIPS machine. The achievement is therefore as much algorithmic as hardware-utilization. | Lowerre & Reddy 1979 Figure 15-1 footnote: "A few times real-time on 100 MIPS processor is anywhere from 200 to 500 MIPSS (Millions of Instructions executed Per Second of Speech). The actual performance of Harpy was about 28 MIPSS, i.e., 80 times real-time on a .35 MIPS processor." | **Green** — verbatim. |
| Per-speaker tuning required 20-30 sentences per talker, against the Final Specification's "slight tuning" target. The CMU 1977 wording is "20-30 sentences per talker"; Lowerre & Reddy 1979 calls this "substantial tuning." Both are anchored; prose should use the CMU 1977 phrasing. | CMU 1977 Figure 1 (PDF p.8); Lowerre & Reddy 1979 Figure 15-1. | **Green** for both phrasings; conflict-noted. |
| HEARSAY-II, also at CMU, was tested on the same 1,011-word vocabulary task. On the simple AIX05 grammar (the same one HARPY used) it achieved approximately 16% semantic error; on the more complex AIX15 grammar it achieved 26% semantic error. It ran 2-20× slower than HARPY. | CMU 1977 Section "The Hearsay-II System" (PDF p.13). | **Green** — verified by `pdftotext` extraction. |
| HEARSAY-II therefore did not meet the Newell 1971 Final Specifications, which had set the target at less than 10% semantic error. The CMU 1977 report does not editorialize on this; the numbers do. | CMU 1977 Figure 1 (HARPY achieved 5%) plus PDF p.13 (HEARSAY-II's 16%/26%) compared against the Newell 1971 Final Specification's <10% target. | **Green** — the comparison is unambiguous from the numbers. |
| BBN's HWIM (Hear What I Mean) and SDC's system also did not meet the Newell 1971 Final Specifications at the September 1976 demonstration. | Juang & Rabiner 2005 PDF p.9: "Neither Hearsay-II nor HWIM (Hear What I Mean) met the DARPA program's performance goal at its conclusion in 1976." Klatt 1977 (the canonical four-system review) is cited but not in hand. | Yellow for the specific HWIM and SDC numbers; Green for the framing claim that only HARPY met the targets. |
| HARPY's design lineage: Hearsay-I (1972, best-first search with backtracking) → Dragon (1974, James K. Baker's CMU statistical system using Markov-network knowledge representation) → Hearsay-II and HARPY (1975-1976). HARPY adopted Dragon's integrated network representation but dropped its a-priori transition probabilities. | CMU 1977 Section "The Dragon System" and "The Harpy System" (PDF p.11-12); Lowerre & Reddy 1979 Section 15-2 ("the single most important intellectual legacy upon which Harpy is based is the representation and delayed-decision techniques first used effectively in the Dragon system"). | **Green** — both anchored. |
| The methodological choreography Newell 1971 §8.6 had proposed — same task, same evaluator, comparable numbers across multiple contractor systems — *worked* at the September 1976 demonstration. ARPA could compare HARPY's 5% semantic error against HEARSAY-II's 16% against HWIM's and SDC's results, on identical task definitions. | Newell 1971 §8.6 (PDF p.50) plus the CMU 1977 cross-system comparison (PDF p.13) plus the implied cross-contractor evaluation that the four-system framing in Klatt 1977 / Juang & Rabiner 2005 documents. | **Green** for the methodological framing. |

**Scene-level note:** This scene is where the chapter's institutional thesis pays off for the first time — the methodology Newell 1971 §8.6 proposed actually produced comparable cross-contractor numbers in 1976. The numbers themselves are well-anchored at CMU's two primary sources (1977 report; 1979 chapter). Avoid prose that frames the 1976 demonstration as a failure overall; HARPY did meet the spec. The "DARPA was disappointed" framing belongs to Scene 5, not here.

## Scene 4 — The IBM Parallel Track

Anchored layer: *700-1,000 words: The IBM parallel track* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Throughout the 1970s, IBM's Continuous Speech Recognition Group at the Thomas J. Watson Research Center, Yorktown Heights NY — led by Frederick Jelinek and including Lalit Bahl, Robert Mercer, J. K. Baker (after he moved from CMU), and others — pursued speech recognition on a track *parallel* to the DARPA SUR program, not within it. | Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 byline and Acknowledgment (p.179, p.190); Juang & Rabiner 2005 PDF p.10 ("In parallel to the ARPA-initiated efforts… IBM's effort, led by Fred Jelinek"). | **Green** — verified by both primary and secondary anchors. |
| The 1983 *IEEE Transactions on PAMI* paper formulates speech recognition not as a problem of grammar but as one of maximum-likelihood decoding over a noisy communication channel: a text generator produces words; an acoustic channel converts those words to a noisy signal; a linguistic decoder must recover the most likely word string from the signal. | Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 abstract (p.179) and §III; Figure 2 "The communication theory view of speech recognition" (p.180). | **Green** — verified verbatim. |
| Within this framework, statistical Markov-source models — sequences of word-emitting state transitions — replace hand-coded linguistic rules. Parameters are estimated from data via the Forward-Backward Algorithm, an iterative method derived from Baum-Welch reestimation. | Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 §IV (Markov source modeling, p.181 ff.) and §VII (Automatic Estimation of Markov Source Parameters, p.185 ff.). | **Green** — verified. |
| The 1983 paper introduced *perplexity* as a task-difficulty measure: "Although vocabulary size is almost always mentioned in the description of an artificial task, by itself it is practically useless as a measure of difficulty. In this section we describe perplexity, a measure of difficulty based on well established information theoretic principles." Perplexity is the exponential of entropy; for the Raleigh Language used by the IBM group, perplexity was 7.27 against a vocabulary of several hundred words. | Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 §IX (pp.187-188), including the explicit definition and the Raleigh-language perplexity number. | **Green** — verified verbatim. |
| The IBM group deliberately ran experiments on the CMU-AIX05 task — the same task CMU used for HARPY to meet the ARPA specifications — to compare statistical decoding against the SUR contractor results. The 1983 paper acknowledges this directly: "The CMU-AIX05 task is the task used by Carnegie-Mellon University in their Speech Understanding System to meet the ARPA specifications." | Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 §X p.189. | **Green** — verbatim. |
| The IBM team's view, as Jelinek's 2005 retrospective documents, was that linguistic intuition could *inform* model structure but parameter values had to come from data: "Linguistic intuition combined with ability to extract information will determine the structure of models and their parameterization. Parameter values will be estimated from (annotated) data. We will rely on advice of linguists to create resources." | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.3-4 ("The View of the IBM Group" slide). | **Green** — verbatim. Useful for tempering any "IBM rejected linguistics" overclaim. |
| Jelinek's slides report a striking accuracy comparison on a "New Raleigh Language" task: phonetic baseforms with expert-estimated statistics achieved 35% accuracy; phonetic baseforms with automatically estimated (data-driven) statistics achieved 75%; orthographic baseforms with automatically estimated statistics achieved 43%. The numbers should be cited as Jelinek transmits them, not as freestanding 1970s experimental results — the exact dataset and year are not specified on the slide. | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.3-4 ("When Linguists Left the Group" slide). | **Green** for the numbers as transmitted. |
| Within SUR itself, the only statistical-method exception was Jim Baker's CMU Dragon system, which Jelinek's 2005 slide explicitly identifies as the SUR-period statistical exception. Baker subsequently moved to IBM (where his name appears in the 1983 PAMI paper's Acknowledgment). | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.3 ("ARPA project on ASR (1971-1976) dominated by AI (except for Jim Baker at CMU)"); Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 Acknowledgment listing "J. K. Baker." | **Green** — both anchored. |

**Scene-level note:** This scene's narrative job is to establish that the IBM-statistical effort was *parallel* to SUR, not within it, and to introduce the methodological vocabulary (maximum likelihood, Markov sources, perplexity, automatic parameter estimation) that will inherit Newell 1971 §8.6's measurement choreography in the next scene. Avoid prose that makes the IBM-statistical methods sound inevitable or victorious in the 1970s; they were compute-bound and not yet decisively winning common-task evaluations until the late-1980s/1990s revival.

## Scene 5 — The Quote and the Methodology That Outlived SUR

Anchored layer: *800-1,100 words: The methodology that outlived SUR (1976-1988-2005)* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| At the September 1976 demonstration, HARPY met the Newell 1971 Final Specifications, but DARPA did not extend SUR funding into a follow-on program. Church 2018 characterizes the period that followed as "a funding winter from 1975 to 1986, where there was no US research funding for machine translation or speech recognition." | Church 2018 NLE tribute to Charles Wayne. | **Green** — verbatim. |
| The funding winter did not stop the IBM statistical-ASR work, which had not been DARPA-funded in the first place. Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 — the canonical statistical-ASR primary source — was published seven years into the funding winter. | Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983 publication date (March 1983, PAMI-5(2)). | **Green**. |
| In 1985, at an Arden House workshop, Robert Mercer uttered the methodological maxim "There is no data like more data" — Jelinek's 2005 slide attributes both the line and the venue. | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.5. | **Green** for the Arden House 1985 attribution. |
| In December 1988, Jelinek delivered a talk titled "Applying Information Theoretic Methods: Evaluation of Grammar Quality" at the Workshop on Evaluation of NLP Systems, Wayne PA — the venue and date Jelinek's 2005 LREC slide identifies as the origin of the famous "fire a linguist" quote: "Whenever I fire a linguist our system performance improves." | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.2 ("The Quote" slide). | **Green** — Jelinek himself documents the December 1988 / Wayne PA provenance. |
| In the mid-1980s, Charles Wayne — at DARPA — restarted US speech-and-language research funding by introducing what became known as the "common task method": well-defined objective evaluation, neutral evaluator (NIST), shared datasets. Church 2018 emphasizes that Wayne's methodological revival is what restarted DARPA-funded ASR research after the funding winter. | Church 2018 NLE tribute to Charles Wayne. | **Green** — verified by WebFetch 2026-04-28. |
| Wayne's mid-1980s methodology is structurally a return to Newell 1971 §8.6's "Public Data and Public Analysis" proposal. The chapter's central infrastructure-history claim is that the SUR program's most durable contribution was not the contractor systems (HARPY, HEARSAY-II, HWIM, SDC) but the *measurement methodology* that Newell named in 1971 and that Wayne re-institutionalized fifteen years later. | Newell 1971 §8.6 (PDF p.50) compared structurally to Wayne's "common task method" (Church 2018). | **Green** for the structural-return framing — both endpoints are anchored at primary or secondary text. |
| Under the Wayne-era common-task framework, statistical methods began to dominate. Juang & Rabiner 2005 frames this directly: "The success of statistical methods revived the interest from DARPA at the juncture of the 1980's and the 1990's, leading to several new speech recognition systems including the Sphinx system from CMU, the BYBLOS system from BBN and the DECIPHER system from SRI. CMU's Sphinx system successfully integrated the statistical method of hidden Markov models with the network search strength of the earlier Harpy system." | Juang & Rabiner 2005 PDF p.15. | **Green** — verbatim. |
| The methodological inheritance flows visibly through publication-pattern data. ACL 1990 had 39 papers, of which 1 used statistical methods. ACL 2003 had 62 papers, of which 48 used statistical methods. | Jelinek 2005 LREC slides PDF p.4 ("Data Driven Parsing" slide). | **Green** — verbatim. |
| Honest close: the SUR contractor systems (HARPY, HEARSAY-II, HWIM, SDC) are now mostly retired as research artifacts. Jim Baker's Dragon lineage led to commercial Dragon Systems and on into Dragon Naturally Speaking; CMU's HARPY-Sphinx lineage led to the open-source CMU Sphinx toolkit; IBM's Continuous Speech Recognition Group lineage led through Tangora and ViaVoice. The chapter's claim is that what *propagated* from SUR was not any specific contractor system but the methodology Newell named in §8.6 and that Wayne re-institutionalized. | Newell 1971 §8.6 (PDF p.50); Juang & Rabiner 2005 PDF p.15 (statistical-method revival lineage); Church 2018 (Wayne's institutional revival). | **Green** for the anchored portions; the specific commercial-product lineage references (Dragon Naturally Speaking, ViaVoice, CMU Sphinx) are background knowledge not anchored at page level — keep them sparse in prose. |

**Scene-level note:** This is where the chapter's thesis lands. The narrative move is "SUR's measurement methodology outlived its contractor systems." Avoid prose that overstates the AI-defeat / statistics-victory framing — within SUR, Baker's Dragon was already statistical; HARPY itself was hybrid; the IBM-statistical track ran in parallel without DARPA funding for over a decade. The chapter's payoff is institutional, not algorithmic.

## Cross-scene notes

- No invented dialogue. Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source (Newell 1971 report; Bahl/Jelinek/Mercer 1983; Jelinek 2005 LREC slides; CMU 1977 SUR Summary; Lowerre & Reddy 1979 chapter) or a transmitted-verbatim secondary (Church 2018 NLE; Juang & Rabiner 2005).
- Where a beat has a Yellow anchor, prose should hedge appropriately ("Klatt 1977 reports…", "as Jelinek transmits the Pierce text…") rather than asserting the claim as direct fact.
- The "fire a linguist" quote is December 1988, not 1971-1976. Any prose that retrojects it into the SUR period is wrong.
- The four SUR contractor systems are HARPY, HEARSAY-II, BBN HWIM, SDC. CMU Dragon was a precursor system within the CMU SUR contract that fed into HARPY's design; do not list it as a fifth contractor system.
- The chapter should NOT include an explainer of HMMs, Markov chains, Viterbi decoding, or the Forward-Backward Algorithm beyond a brief naming-and-purpose introduction. Mathematical detail belongs to Part 5 (HMM math chapters) and Part 7 (deep-learning ASR chapters), not Ch32.
