# Open Questions: Chapter 9 — The Memory Miracle

Surfaced from `brief.md` *Conflict Notes* and the Yellow/Red entries in `sources.md`. Each question identifies the specific evidence needed to resolve it. Drafting cannot rely on any open-question item until its evidence is in hand.

## Resolution Required Before Drafting

### Q1. The Wang / Way-Dong Woo co-inventorship

- **Question:** Was the 1949 magnetic-core "pulse transfer controlling device" (US Patent 2,708,722) the work of An Wang alone, or jointly of Wang and Way-Dong Woo at Harvard's Computation Laboratory?
- **Why it matters:** Multiple secondary sources (Wikipedia *Magnetic-core memory*; Computer History Museum chronologies) credit "Wang and Woo." The patent face-sheet names only Wang. If the chapter's Scene 4 patent-war section asserts co-inventorship, it needs a primary anchor; if it does not, it should explicitly note the secondary-source attribution to Woo and explain why the chapter follows the patent record.
- **Evidence needed:** An Wang's autobiography *Lessons* (1986, Addison-Wesley), specifically the chapter on the Harvard Computation Laboratory years; Harvard Computation Laboratory archival records; Howard Aiken's correspondence (Aiken was the lab's director).
- **Status:** Yellow (the patent is anchored, the secondary attribution is widespread but not primary-sourced). Do not assert co-inventorship in prose without a primary anchor; do explicitly note the disputed attribution in a footnote.

### Q2. IBM's 1956 purchase of the Wang patent — exact figure and conditions

- **Question:** Was the IBM purchase of US Patent 2,708,722 from An Wang exactly $500,000, and what were the licensing conditions? Did the purchase include cross-licensing of any IBM patents to Wang Laboratories?
- **Why it matters:** Scene 4's "patent war" closing beat depends on this transaction as the commercial resolution of the Forrester / Wang / Rajchman priority picture. The $500,000 figure is widely cited in tertiary sources but lacks a single primary anchor. If the figure is wrong by an order of magnitude, the closing beat changes character.
- **Evidence needed:** Pugh 1984 *Memories That Shaped an Industry* page anchor; Bashe et al. 1986 *IBM's Early Computers* page anchor; IBM Archives; Wang's autobiography.
- **Status:** Yellow. The chapter can land the beat with the $500,000 figure caveated as "approximately" if Pugh 1984 cannot be accessed; better to anchor it.

### Q3. First Whirlwind core memory installation — precise date

- **Question:** On what specific date in summer 1953 was the first 32×32×16-bit magnetic-core memory installed on the Whirlwind I computer?
- **Why it matters:** Tertiary sources (CHM "1953: Whirlwind computer debuts core memory"; Wikipedia *Magnetic-core memory*) cite "August 1953"; Forrester's 1994 oral history is qualitative ("within a couple of months after that, we moved it into the Whirlwind computer"). For Scene 5's commercialization arc to land with date precision, a primary anchor is needed.
- **Evidence needed:** Redmond & Smith 1980 *Project Whirlwind* page anchor; MIT Archives Project Whirlwind Collection (MC 665); the original Memory Test Computer reports from the 1953 series of MIT Digital Computer Laboratory technical reports.
- **Status:** Yellow. Acceptable to land the chapter with "summer 1953" if Pugh 1984 / Redmond & Smith 1980 cannot be accessed.

### Q4. The Wang/Forrester/Rajchman priority dispute — court versus license outcome

- **Question:** Did the Forrester (Research Corporation) / Wang / Rajchman (RCA) patent dispute ever produce a court ruling on technical priority, or was it resolved entirely through commercial licensing?
- **Why it matters:** Scene 4's stance is that the priority picture was settled commercially (IBM's 1956 purchase of Wang's patent) rather than judicially. If there was a court ruling that the chapter is overlooking, that stance needs revision.
- **Evidence needed:** Pugh 1984 *Memories That Shaped an Industry*; legal-history coverage in *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing*.
- **Status:** Yellow. Plausible that the dispute never reached priority adjudication and was resolved entirely through licensing; needs primary confirmation.

## Lower-Priority Questions (Do Not Block Drafting)

### Q5. The German tank-turret magnetic-amplifier provenance

- **Question:** Did Forrester actually encounter the rectangular-hysteresis magnetic materials in 1949 advertisements derived from German wartime electronics, or is this a 1994 reconstructed memory imposing a tidier narrative on the 1949 materials-search process?
- **Why it matters:** Minor — the 1949 dating of the magnetic-material insight is corroborated independently by the IEEE Pioneers entry; the German provenance is colorful but not load-bearing. The chapter can mention it as Forrester's recollection without anchoring the historical accuracy of the wartime-electronics origin story.
- **Evidence needed:** Wartime German electronics technical literature; or earlier Forrester accounts that pre-date the 1994 oral history (his 1991 *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing* article on Whirlwind, if extant).
- **Status:** Yellow, non-blocking. Frame the German-tank-turret detail as Forrester's recollection, not as historical fact.

### Q6. End-date of magnetic-core memory dominance

- **Question:** When did integrated-circuit semiconductor RAM displace magnetic-core memory as the primary internal-memory technology in commercial computers? Forrester's 1994 oral history places the displacement in the early 1980s; most secondary sources cite the mid-1970s.
- **Why it matters:** Minor — the chapter's window closes around 1956. Scene 5's closing beat would mention magnetic-core's dominance in passing; the precise end-date is not load-bearing.
- **Evidence needed:** Pugh 1984; tertiary cross-checks across multiple sources.
- **Status:** Yellow, non-blocking. State as a range (mid-1970s through early 1980s) rather than a single date.

### Q7. Manufacturing labor in the 1953-1956 era

- **Question:** Who actually wove the early 1953-1956 coincident-current core memory arrays at IBM Poughkeepsie and at MIT Lincoln Laboratory? Were they women? Were they women specifically chosen for textile-handicraft skill, as the Apollo-era Raytheon "Little Old Ladies" pattern suggests for the 1960s?
- **Why it matters:** The chapter's existing brief deliberately constrains the manual-weaving scene to a brief mention pending primary archival photos. The popular labor history is well-anchored to the 1960s Apollo-era Raytheon pattern (Rosner et al. 2018 CHI); the 1953-1956 era is less anchored. The chapter must not import the Apollo labor history into the earlier era.
- **Evidence needed:** IBM Poughkeepsie production-photograph archives; MIT Lincoln Laboratory production records; oral histories from IBM 704 production-line workers (CHM oral history collection).
- **Status:** Yellow with strong caveat. The chapter mentions the 1953-1956 manufacturing labor story briefly per the existing brief; do not expand pending primary anchors specific to the era.

### Q8. The exact Rajchman / RCA core-memory patent that maps to the *Myriabit* paper

- **Question:** Among Rajchman's many RCA core-memory patents (US 2,667,542; US 2,673,337; US 2,889,540 and others), which specific patent corresponds to the technical content of the October 1953 *Proc. IRE* "Myriabit" paper?
- **Why it matters:** Minor — Scene 4 currently cites the Rajchman 1953 paper as the primary anchor for RCA's parallel work; a specific patent number would tighten the patent-war scene's symmetry between Forrester (US 2,736,880), Wang (US 2,708,722), and Rajchman.
- **Evidence needed:** Rajchman's RCA patent portfolio; cross-reference against the *Myriabit* paper's technical content.
- **Status:** Red, non-blocking. The Rajchman 1953 paper anchors RCA's work without requiring a specific patent.
