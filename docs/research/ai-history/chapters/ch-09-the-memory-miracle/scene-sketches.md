## Scene 1 — The Amnesiac Machines

Anchored layer: *600-900 words: The amnesiac machines* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| The 1948 Manchester rig that demonstrated the von Neumann stored-program architecture in practice was, by its authors' own description, "purely experimental, and is on too small a scale to be of mathematical value … built primarily to test the soundness of the storage principle employed." | Williams & Kilburn 1948 *Nature* 162:487 first paragraph, verified by Claude WebFetch + HTML extraction 2026-04-28. | **Green** |
| The storage principle being tested — the Williams-tube electrostatic CRT memory — had a short-term retention "of the order of 0.2 seconds" provided passively "by the insulating properties of the screen material." | Kilburn 1947 internal report (Manchester transcription), Summary section verbatim. | **Green** — Claude verified 2026-04-28. |
| Long-term storage required active regeneration of the charge pattern at "a frequency greater than 5 cycles/second." | Kilburn 1947 report, Summary section verbatim. | **Green** |
| The hypothetical machine described in the 1947 report could "set up and obey" an instruction in 600 microseconds in series mode. | Kilburn 1947 report, Summary section verbatim. | **Green** |
| The contemporary alternative was the mercury acoustic delay line — a meter-long mercury tube with piezoelectric crystals at each end, storing roughly a thousand binary digits in a roughly one-millisecond transit time, accessible only when the bit reached the far end. | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim description. | **Green** as a Forrester recollection of the contemporary alternatives. |
| Forrester's later characterization of the electrostatic-CRT memory that Whirlwind originally used: "expensive and short-lived and not very reliable." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| The chapter's framing claim: a stored-program architecture that forgets itself in 0.2 seconds is not a stored-program architecture in any operational sense — it is a circuit that is regenerating its own memory hundreds of times per second and trusting that the regeneration is faithful. | Synthesis from Kilburn 1947 + Williams & Kilburn 1948. | **Green** as a synthesis of two Green anchors. |

**Scene-level note:** The chapter's opening should resist dramatizing the failure modes (the "passing streetcar" anecdote is widely repeated tertiary but lacks a single primary anchor). The 0.2-second retention figure from the 1947 Kilburn report is dramatic enough on its own without invented atmospherics. Frame the scene as engineering specification, not as ghost-story.

## Scene 2 — Project Whirlwind's Memory Crisis

Anchored layer: *700-1,000 words: Project Whirlwind's memory crisis* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| The Whirlwind project began in 1944 as an analog computer for aircraft stability and control under the Navy Special Devices Center; pivoted to digital in 1946 in conversation with Perry Crawford after a year of analog work that "was not going to be satisfactory." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim ("Officially, the work began in late 1944 and the analog computer part of it ran through 1945, and the shift over to serial digital computers was roughly 1946"). | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| In 1948, Forrester wrote two memoranda proposing digital computers for combat information centers, integrating data from aircraft, ships, and submarines. This pivot is what made memory reliability a project-killing constraint rather than an annoyance. | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| The Soviet Union's August 1949 atomic-bomb test catalyzed the air-defense study (Project Charles) that pulled Whirlwind into the SAGE trajectory. | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| Forrester (Concord 1994 oral history) characterized the late-1940s memory landscape: "people were desperate for memory for computers. All kinds of things were being tried." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| Forrester recalled that the team "seriously considered renting a television link from Boston to Buffalo and back so that we could store binary digits in the transit time that it would take to make the round-trip on the television channel." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection — useful as a measure of the desperation. |
| Whirlwind's reliability emphasis distinguished it from the era's other digital computer projects, which "were devoted to scientific computation where if the machine stopped working, you could begin over or do the job tomorrow." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |

**Scene-level note:** This scene's narrative job is to explain why the *kind* of computing Whirlwind was attempting (real-time control loops with safety-critical outputs) made the Williams-tube failure modes that scientific batch computers tolerated into a project-killing problem. Resist the temptation to make this a Forrester-as-genius-engineer scene. The thesis is institutional: a different funding pipeline (Navy → Air Force → Lincoln Laboratory) created a different reliability requirement, which created the demand for a different memory technology.

## Scene 3 — The Three-Dimensional Idea

Anchored layer: *700-1,000 words: The three-dimensional idea* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| In 1947 Forrester thought through what a three-dimensional storage device could look like: "if we had one-dimensional storage and two-dimensional storage, what's the possibility of a three-dimensional storage. In thinking about that in about 1947, I arrived at a logical structure that satisfied the idea, but it was built around devices that were themselves not really practical." The devices were neon glow-discharge tubes; the team did individual-unit tests but "didn't really pursue it." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. Cross-confirmed by IEEE Computer Society Pioneers entry: "1947: Developed initial random-access storage concept using glow-discharge cells." | **Green** for both the anecdote and the 1947 dating. |
| The 1947 logical structure already had the key property that would later make magnetic-core memory practical: coincident-current selection. "You could activate a wire say in the 'x' axis and another one crossing in the 'y' axis and only the glow tube at the intersection would have enough voltage on it to break down and begin to discharge." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection of the logical structure. |
| Glow tubes failed because their "characteristics … change with temperature and age" — drift made them unsuitable for memory devices that needed to discriminate between two specific states reliably over time. | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| In 1949 Forrester recognized that magnetic materials with rectangular hysteresis (used by the German army for tank-turret magnetic amplifiers in WWII) could substitute for the impractical glow tubes: "Over a period of two or three months we developed how that could be done and then over the next three years or so, we in fact brought it to the point where it was a working, permanently reliable system." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. Cross-confirmed by IEEE Pioneers: "1949: Reconceived the approach as 'toroidal, random-access, coincident-current magnetic storage'." | **Green** for the 1949 dating and the substitution insight. Yellow for the German-tank-turret provenance specifically (single-source recollection). |
| Forrester published the technical case in *Journal of Applied Physics* 22(1), pp. 44-48 (January 1951): "Tests show that most existing metallic magnetic materials switch in 20 to 10,000 microseconds and are too slow. Nonmetallic magnetic materials … switch in less than a microsecond." The architecture required "Only one magnetic core per binary digit" and used a 2:1 magnetizing-force discrimination ratio. | Forrester 1951 *J. Appl. Phys.* abstract verbatim, verified by Claude WebFetch against ADS Harvard mirror 2026-04-28. DOI 10.1063/1.1699817. | **Green** for abstract-level claims. |
| Forrester filed US Patent 2,736,880 ("Multicoordinate Digital Information Storage Device") on May 11, 1951; the patent was assigned to Research Corporation, New York. The patent specified rings "of magnetic material, each having substantially rectangular hysteresis properties … the several rings being arranged in rows and columns" and demonstrated that a 3D array requires only "3 ∛number of cores" input leads. The patent did not issue until February 28, 1956 — four years and nine months after filing. | US Patent 2,736,880 face-sheet, verified Claude WebFetch on Google Patents 2026-04-28. | **Green** |

**Scene-level note:** This is the scene where the *idea* arrives but the *industry* hasn't yet. The 1951 *J. Appl. Phys.* paper is a technical-case paper, not a deployment paper; the 1951-1956 patent gap reflects the slow pace of US Patent Office examination plus the parallel filings by Wang and Rajchman that complicated the priority picture. The scene should foreground that the 1951 paper is the field's defining technical statement, but resist asserting that Forrester "invented" magnetic-core memory — Wang's pulse-transfer patent already existed (filed October 1949, eighteen months before Forrester's filing) and Rajchman's RCA work was running on a parallel track.

## Scene 4 — The Patent War

Anchored layer: *500-800 words: The patent war* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Jan A. Rajchman at RCA Laboratories Camden NJ had developed the Selectron tube in 1946-1947 — the first random-access electrostatic-storage tube, 256 bits per tube, used in the JOHNNIAC at RAND. He then pursued a parallel coincident-current core path, shaping his prototype cores using a converted aspirin-tablet press. | IEEE Computer Society Pioneers entry on Rajchman, verified by Claude WebFetch 2026-04-28. | **Green** for the career arc and aspirin-press anecdote. |
| Rajchman published "A Myriabit Magnetic-Core Matrix Memory" in *Proceedings of the IRE* 41(10), pp. 1407-1421 (October 1953), describing RCA's 10,000-bit matrix and coining the term "myriabit." | Rajchman 1953 *Proc. IRE* bibliographic citation; full text behind IEEE Xplore paywall. | **Green** for the bibliographic citation; Yellow for body-text claims. |
| At Harvard's Computation Laboratory, An Wang filed US Patent 2,708,722 ("Pulse Transfer Controlling Device") on October 21, 1949 — eighteen months before Forrester's 1951 filing. The patent specified magnetic materials with residual flux density "at least 0.4-0.5, preferably greater than 0.80" of saturation, and described a write-after-read cycle that made coincident-current core RAM operationally viable. | US Patent 2,708,722 face-sheet, verified Claude WebFetch on Google Patents 2026-04-28. | **Green** |
| Multiple secondary sources name Way-Dong Woo as Wang's co-inventor at the Harvard Computation Laboratory; Patent 2,708,722's face-sheet names Wang alone. The chapter does not assert co-inventorship pending resolution (Wang autobiography or Harvard archival records). | Patent face-sheet (Green); Wikipedia *Magnetic-core memory* tertiary attribution to Wang + Woo (Yellow). | Yellow attribution; tracked as `open-questions.md` Q1. |
| Wang's patent issued May 17, 1955 — six years after the 1949 application. Forrester's patent issued February 28, 1956 — four years and nine months after the 1951 application. The long pendency reflects the parallel-filing complications. | US Patents 2,708,722 and 2,736,880 face-sheets. | **Green** |
| The patent dispute resolved commercially in 1956 when IBM purchased Wang's patent for approximately $500,000 — a transaction that took the priority question out of the courts and into the licensing market. | Wikipedia *Magnetic-core memory* tertiary cite of Bashe et al. 1986; primary anchor in IBM Archives or Pugh 1984 pending. | Yellow |
| The chapter's stance on inventorship: three parallel paths (Forrester at MIT, Rajchman at RCA, Wang/Woo at Harvard) developed magnetic-core memory in the 1949-1953 window. Industrial dominance came through MIT's institutional pipeline (Memory Test Computer → Whirlwind → SAGE → IBM 704), not through technical primacy alone. | Synthesis. | **Green** as a synthesis of three patent-anchored paths plus one anchored commercial outcome. |

**Scene-level note:** This is the scene where the chapter most needs to resist single-inventor framing. The popular narrative places Forrester as "the inventor of core memory"; the patent record places Wang's filing first; Rajchman's published RCA work was concurrent. All three are anchored. The chapter's job is to present the priority picture as parallel rather than sequential, and to make clear that the industrial outcome was settled by licensing economics (IBM's 1956 purchase of Wang's patent) rather than by patent-court priority decisions.

## Scene 5 — Commercialization

Anchored layer: *600-900 words: Commercialization* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| To validate the magnetic-core memory at full scale before transplanting it into Whirlwind, Norman Taylor (chief engineer) and Kenneth Olsen (research assistant) proposed building a "Memory Test Computer" out of the team's existing test-equipment building blocks. Forrester recalled: "I must say I doubted that they could do it and certainly not in the nine months they said it would take, but they came very close." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| The Memory Test Computer succeeded; Forrester then "moved it into the Whirlwind computer to replace the electrostatic storage tubes that we had been using for memory but which were expensive and short-lived and not very reliable." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| The first magnetic-core memory installed on Whirlwind (summer 1953) was a 32×32×16-bit array — 16 stacked planes of 1,024 cores each. Cycle time was approximately 9 microseconds, vs the displaced CRT system's ~25 microseconds. | Wikipedia *Magnetic-core memory* and CHM "1953: Whirlwind computer debuts core memory" tertiary citations of Bashe et al. 1986. | Yellow — primary anchor in Bashe 1986 or Redmond & Smith 1980 needed. |
| With magnetic core proven on Whirlwind, MIT Lincoln Laboratory selected IBM as the production manufacturer for the SAGE air-defense computers. Forrester recalled the selection process: "we sent out requests to a substantial number of companies, probably 15 or so" and "it was clear that IBM was far ahead of any of the others as a possible company to do the work, so we recommended them to the Air Force." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection of the selection process. |
| The production SAGE computer (IBM AN/FSQ-7) was a heavily-modified version of the Whirlwind/Memory Test Computer architecture. The SAGE network grew to 30-some control centers across North America, each with 60,000-80,000 vacuum tubes in a 4-story building roughly 160 feet square, operating from the mid-late 1950s into the early 1980s with approximately 99.8% availability. | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection of the production-system shape; Yellow for the operational figures as historical fact. |
| The closing date marker: "By 1956, the SAGE air defense system was essentially cast in its direction. The first of 30-some computer centers was nearing completion in New Jersey." | Forrester Concord 1994 oral history verbatim. | **Green** as Forrester recollection. |
| The commercial spin-out was the IBM 704 (announced 1954, first delivered April 1955, 123 units produced 1955-1960) — the first mass-produced commercial computer with magnetic-core memory standard, replacing the Williams-tube storage of the IBM 701 predecessor. 36-bit word, 4,096-word standard core capacity in the 737 Magnetic Core Storage Unit. | Wikipedia *IBM 704* tertiary citation of Bashe et al. 1986. | Yellow — primary anchor in Bashe 1986 or IBM Archives needed. |
| The chapter's closing thesis (forward-pointer to Ch11): by the time of the Dartmouth Summer Research Project on Artificial Intelligence (June-August 1956), the symbolic-AI agenda had inherited a memory infrastructure that was no longer the load-bearing constraint. The von Neumann architecture's memory problem was solved; the next bottleneck was software. | Synthesis. Cross-link Ch11. | **Green** as a chapter-closing synthesis of the anchored material above. |

**Scene-level note:** This is where the chapter's thesis lands. The narrative move is "the memory crisis got solved by physical engineering, on the SAGE/IBM commercialization timeline, just in time for the symbolic-AI agenda to assume reliable memory as a starting point." Don't overstate — the chapter must not claim that SAGE caused the AI summer or that Dartmouth would have failed without core memory. The pattern is *enabling*: by 1956 the von Neumann architecture had its physical substrate, and the historical foreground for the next chapters can shift from hardware constraints to software ambitions.

## Cross-scene notes

- No invented dialogue. Forrester's Concord oral history is rich and quotable; quote it as Forrester's recollection rather than as third-person fact.
- Where secondary sources conflict (e.g., on the Wang/Woo co-inventorship, on the precise date of first Whirlwind core installation, on the end-date of magnetic-core dominance), the chapter should name the disagreement rather than picking a side.
- "Single-inventor" framing is dismantled across Scene 4; do not let any scene accidentally re-introduce it.
- The chapter should NOT include an explainer of CRT physics, magnetic hysteresis as a general concept, or vacuum-tube reliability theory. Those belong in earlier chapters or in supporting material. Ch9's narrow focus is the *substitution event*: one bad memory technology displaced by a much-less-bad one, with the institutional and patent details that made the substitution stick.
- The "woven by hand by female textile workers" labor history belongs primarily to 1960s Apollo *core rope memory* (Raytheon, Waltham MA — Rosner et al. 2018 CHI), which is out of scope for this chapter. The 1953-1956 *core RAM* manufacturing labor story is mentioned briefly with explicit constraint per the existing brief; do not import the Apollo labor story into the Whirlwind/IBM 704 era.
