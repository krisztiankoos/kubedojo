# Brief: Chapter 9 — The Memory Miracle

## Thesis

The von Neumann stored-program architecture (Ch8) is a logical idea, not a physical one. It worked on paper and in a small experimental Manchester rig (Williams & Kilburn 1948 *Nature* 162:487) only because the rig's authors were willing to live with the storage system's brutal operating limits — short-term retention "of the order of 0.2 seconds" requiring active regeneration at greater than 5 cycles per second (Kilburn 1947 internal report, Summary). For the architecture to leave the laboratory and hold a complex program for hours rather than fractions of a second, somebody had to invent a memory technology that didn't forget on its own. Magnetic-core memory — independently developed in roughly the same window (1949-1953) by Jay Forrester at MIT, Jan Rajchman at RCA, and An Wang (with Way-Dong Woo) at Harvard's Computation Laboratory — was that invention. Its eventual industrial dominance, secured through MIT's Whirlwind, the SAGE air defense program's IBM AN/FSQ-7 production run, and IBM's commercial 704 mainframe, is what made the symbolic-AI era of 1956+ infrastructurally possible. The chapter's frame is honest engineering: this is not a story of a discovery, but of a switch from one bad memory technology to a much-less-bad one, accompanied by a parallel patent war and an industrial commercialization arc.

## Scope

- **IN SCOPE:** the operating-limit characterization of Williams-tube electrostatic CRT storage and mercury delay lines as load-bearing memory in 1948-1951 stored-program machines; Jay Forrester's path from glow-discharge cells (1947) to magnetic-core arrays (1949-1953) at MIT, including the Project Whirlwind context and the role of magnetic-amplifier materials from German wartime electronics; the parallel work of Jan Rajchman at RCA (Selectron tube 1947 → myriabit core matrix 1953) and An Wang (with Way-Dong Woo) at the Harvard Computation Laboratory (1949 patent application); the resulting priority dispute and patent war; the commercialization arc from MIT's Memory Test Computer through the Whirlwind core upgrade (~1953) to MIT Lincoln Laboratory's IBM AN/FSQ-7 production for the SAGE air-defense network (mid-late 1950s) to the commercial IBM 704 (1954-1960).
- **OUT OF SCOPE:** semiconductor RAM (post-1970 displacement of core); Apollo Guidance Computer core *rope* memory and the Raytheon "Little Old Ladies" labor history (these belong to the 1960s and to a different memory technology — see Rosner et al. 2018 CHI); the broader history of SAGE's command-and-control role (Edwards 1996 *The Closed World*); LISP, AI programming languages, and any post-1956 software development (Ch11 onward); IBM 305 RAMAC and disk storage (a different storage layer entirely).

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not present magnetic-core memory as a singular invention by a single person. The historical record is parallel and contested: Forrester's coincident-current 3D architecture, Rajchman's RCA matrix work, and Wang's pulse-transfer cycle were developed in the same 1949-1953 window with overlapping patent claims. The chapter's stance is that the *technology* (toroidal ferrite cores arranged in coincident-current matrices) was an idea whose time had come; the *industrial dominance* of the Forrester variant came through MIT's institutional position, the Memory Test Computer validation, the SAGE contract with IBM, and the Research Corporation's licensing leverage on the Forrester patent — not through technical primacy alone.

The chapter must also not collapse two distinct labor histories. The widely-cited "Little Old Ladies" / female-textile-worker manufacturing story is well-anchored to 1960s *Apollo Guidance Computer core rope memory* (Raytheon, Waltham MA — Rosner et al. 2018 CHI). The 1953-1956 *Whirlwind/IBM 704 era* manufacturing of coincident-current core *RAM* arrays is a separate and less-anchored labor story; the chapter mentions it briefly, deliberately constrained, pending IBM Poughkeepsie or MIT Lincoln archival anchors.

The chapter must not invent dialogue or internal motives for any participant. Forrester's 1994 Concord oral history is rich and quotable but is a recollection given decades after the events; the chapter should attribute its anecdotes (the Boston-Buffalo television-link, the German tank-turret magnetic amplifier, the IBM-from-fifteen-companies survey) to Forrester's recollection rather than asserting them as bare historical fact.

Forward-references are sparse pointers — a single line that the resulting infrastructure made the Dartmouth-era symbolic-AI work possible (cross-link to Ch11). The chapter does not anticipate Ch10 (Imitation Game), Ch12 (Logic Theorist), or any post-1956 development.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Amnesiac Machines.** The 1948 Manchester rig and what it actually was — a "purely experimental" demonstration that a stored-program architecture could be made to work *if* the operator could tolerate a 0.2-second short-term retention and a >5 Hz refresh requirement (Kilburn 1947; Williams & Kilburn 1948 *Nature*). Mercury delay lines (slow but reliable). Williams tubes (faster but volatile). The infrastructure cost of running real programs on hardware that forgot them.
2. **Project Whirlwind's Memory Crisis.** Forrester's MIT Whirlwind project, motivated by a Navy flight-simulator request that pivoted into Cold War air-defense computing. Why a real-time control application can't tolerate the Williams-tube failure modes that a batch scientific computer can. The "desperate for memory" period — and Forrester's own anecdote of considering renting a Boston-to-Buffalo television link as a delay-line memory.
3. **The Three-Dimensional Idea.** Forrester's 1947 logical structure for a coincident-current 3D store, originally implemented with neon glow-discharge tubes (a dead end), and the 1949 substitution of rectangular-hysteresis magnetic materials. The 1951 *Journal of Applied Physics* paper as the field's defining technical statement (switching <1 μs nonmetallic vs 20-10,000 μs metallic; one core per binary digit; 2:1 selection ratio). The 1951 patent filing that would not issue until 1956.
4. **The Patent War.** Jan Rajchman at RCA Camden — Selectron tube 1947, then a parallel coincident-current path culminating in the 1953 *Proc. IRE* "Myriabit" paper. An Wang (with Way-Dong Woo) at the Harvard Computation Laboratory — October 1949 patent application on the pulse-transfer write-after-read cycle. The three parallel paths and the patent disputes that resolved commercially when IBM purchased Wang's patent for approximately $500,000 in 1956. The chapter's stance: parallel invention, not single-inventor primacy.
5. **Commercialization.** The Memory Test Computer (Norman Taylor, Ken Olsen) built from existing test-equipment building blocks in roughly nine months and used to validate the magnetic-core memory at full scale, then transplanted into Whirlwind to replace the failing Williams tubes. The selection of IBM as commercial manufacturer for the SAGE AN/FSQ-7 production run from approximately 15 surveyed companies. The IBM 704 (1954-1960, 123 units) as the first mass-produced commercial computer with magnetic-core memory standard, replacing the Williams tubes of the IBM 701. The infrastructure handoff: by 1956 (the Dartmouth summer; cross-link to Ch11) the symbolic-AI agenda had its memory.

## Conflict Notes

- **Wang / Way-Dong Woo co-inventorship.** Patent 2,708,722 names An Wang as sole inventor; multiple secondary sources name Way-Dong Woo as co-inventor at Harvard's Computation Laboratory. Until resolved (Wang autobiography or Harvard archival records), the chapter attributes the patent to Wang per the face-sheet and notes the disputed co-inventorship in a footnote.
- **Forrester vs Rajchman priority.** Both pursued coincident-current magnetic-core memory in roughly the same 1949-1953 window. The chapter's stance is parallel invention, not winner-picking. Patent disputes resolved commercially through licensing (IBM's 1956 purchase of Wang's patent for ~$500,000) rather than through judicial priority decisions.
- **First Whirlwind core memory installation date.** Multiple tertiary sources cite "August 1953"; the Forrester oral history is qualitative. Pugh 1984 or Redmond & Smith 1980 page anchor needed for the precise date.
- **End-date of magnetic-core dominance.** Forrester's 1994 oral history places it in the early 1980s; most secondary sources cite the mid-1970s. State as a range in prose.
- **Apollo / Raytheon vs Whirlwind / IBM labor history.** The well-anchored "Little Old Ladies" / female-textile-worker manufacturing story belongs to 1960s Apollo *core rope* memory, not 1953-1956 *core RAM*. The chapter's manufacturing scene is deliberately constrained to a brief mention pending primary anchors specific to the 1953-1956 era.
