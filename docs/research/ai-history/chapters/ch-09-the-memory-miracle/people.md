# People: Chapter 9 — The Memory Miracle

Verified protagonist bios. Bios are kept narrowly to the 1947-1956 window relevant to the magnetic-core memory story; later careers (System Dynamics, DEC, Wang Laboratories, RCA executive roles) are out of scope.

## The Three Inventors

### Jay Wright Forrester (1918-2016)
- **Position in 1947-1956:** Director, MIT Digital Computer Laboratory (1946-1951); subsequently Head of Division 6, MIT Lincoln Laboratory, leading the SAGE air-defense system development.
- **Role in the chapter:** The chapter's central engineering protagonist for Scenes 2, 3, and 5. Conceived the coincident-current 3D logical structure in 1947 (initially using neon glow-discharge tubes), substituted magnetic materials in 1949, published the field-defining technical statement in *J. Appl. Phys.* in January 1951, filed US Patent 2,736,880 in May 1951 (issued February 1956), and led the Memory Test Computer / Whirlwind core-upgrade / SAGE / IBM commercialization arc.
- **Why he matters here:** Forrester's institutional position at MIT, combined with the Navy / Air Force funding pipeline that flowed through the Special Devices Center → Office of Naval Research → Project Charles → Lincoln Laboratory, is what turned coincident-current core memory from one of three parallel inventions into the industrial standard. His 1994 Concord (MA) Free Public Library oral history with Renee Garrelick is the chapter's single richest primary source.
- **Verification:** **Green** for career arc (1944 project start, 1947 glow-tube concept, 1949 magnetic-material insight, 1951 *J. Appl. Phys.* publication and patent filing, 1956 patent issue) — anchored to Forrester's 1994 Concord oral history and the IEEE Computer Society Pioneers entry. Forrester's birth (July 14, 1918, Climax NE) and education (BSc Nebraska 1939; SM MIT 1945) anchored to the same IEEE Pioneers entry.

### Jan Aleksander Rajchman (1911-1989)
- **Position in 1947-1956:** Member of technical staff, RCA Laboratories (Camden NJ), 1936-1958.
- **Role in the chapter:** The parallel-invention protagonist for Scene 4. Developed the Selectron tube (1946-1947) — the first random-access electrostatic-storage tube (256 bits per tube) — used in the JOHNNIAC at RAND. Pursued magnetic-core matrix memory in parallel with Forrester at MIT, shaping cores using a converted aspirin-tablet press. Coined the term "myriabit" for a 10,000-bit core matrix in his October 1953 *Proc. IRE* paper.
- **Why he matters here:** Rajchman's parallel work at RCA is what makes Scene 4's "patent war" honestly a war rather than a winner-takes-all narrative. His Selectron tube also gives the chapter a foil for the magnetic-core idea: it solved the same volatility problem with electrostatic storage in a more clever tube, and lost the industrial race because magnetic cores scaled better.
- **Verification:** **Green** for career arc (1936-1958 at RCA Camden; 1946-1947 Selectron tube; 1953 myriabit paper) and for the aspirin-tablet-press anecdote — anchored to the IEEE Computer Society Pioneers entry verified by Claude WebFetch 2026-04-28. Born August 10, 1911 (London); died April 1, 1989 (Princeton NJ).

### An Wang (1920-1990)
- **Position in 1949:** Research fellow at Harvard's Computation Laboratory under Howard Aiken.
- **Role in the chapter:** The third parallel-invention protagonist for Scene 4. Filed US Patent 2,708,722 ("Pulse Transfer Controlling Device") on October 21, 1949 — issued May 17, 1955 — describing a magnetic-core-based device that solved the write-after-read cycle that made coincident-current core RAM operationally viable. Multiple secondary sources name Way-Dong Woo as Wang's co-inventor; the patent face-sheet lists Wang alone (see `open-questions.md` Q1).
- **Why he matters here:** Wang's 1949 patent application predates Forrester's 1951 filing by eighteen months, and IBM eventually purchased the Wang patent for approximately $500,000 in 1956 — a transaction that resolved the patent war commercially rather than judicially. His later career founding Wang Laboratories is out of scope; the chapter's window closes before Wang Laboratories' 1955 incorporation matters to the magnetic-core narrative.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the patent filing/issue dates (Google Patents face-sheet, verified 2026-04-28). Yellow for the Wang/Woo co-inventorship (see Q1).

## The Manchester Pair (Williams-Tube Anchor)

### Frederic Calland Williams (1911-1977)
- **Position in 1947-1948:** Professor of Electrical Engineering, University of Manchester. Co-founder of the Royal Society Computing Machine Laboratory.
- **Role in the chapter:** Scene 1 only. Co-author with Tom Kilburn of the September 1948 *Nature* Letter that announced a working stored-program experimental machine using the Williams-tube electrostatic CRT memory. The 1947 internal report by Kilburn was approved by Williams (signed "1.12.47").
- **Why he matters here:** The Williams tube is named after him. The chapter's opening operating-limit characterization of the Williams tube (0.2-second short-term retention, >5 Hz refresh requirement) is anchored to a report that Williams approved.
- **Verification:** Yellow for biographical specifics; bibliographic anchor for the 1948 *Nature* Letter is **Green**.

### Tom Kilburn (1921-2001)
- **Position in 1947:** Researcher at the University of Manchester Electrical Engineering Department under Williams.
- **Role in the chapter:** Scene 1 only. Author of the December 1, 1947 internal report "A Storage System for Use with Binary Digital Computing Machines" — the chapter's primary anchor for Williams-tube operating limits. Co-author with Williams of the 1948 *Nature* Letter and 1949 *Proc. IEE* published paper.
- **Why he matters here:** The 1947 report is the chapter's single most-load-bearing primary anchor for the "amnesiac machines" scene. It quotes the 0.2-second retention figure, the 5-cycle/second refresh requirement, and the 600 μs hypothetical-machine instruction time — all directly from Kilburn's text.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the 1947 report authorship (signed "Author: Mr. T. KILBURN" in the transcribed text). Yellow for biographical specifics.

## The Whirlwind Engineers (Commercialization)

### Norman H. Taylor
- **Position in early 1950s:** Chief engineer, MIT Digital Computer Laboratory.
- **Role in the chapter:** Scene 5. With Ken Olsen, proposed building the Memory Test Computer "out of test equipment" to validate the magnetic core memory at full scale before transplanting it into Whirlwind. Forrester (Concord oral history): "I must say I doubted that they could do it and certainly not in the nine months they said it would take, but they came very close."
- **Verification:** **Green** for the Memory Test Computer attribution (Forrester Concord oral history verbatim). Yellow for biographical specifics; further anchoring would require Redmond & Smith 1980 or MIT Archives.

### Kenneth H. "Ken" Olsen (1926-2011)
- **Position in early 1950s:** Research assistant working under Norman Taylor at the MIT Digital Computer Laboratory while completing his master's degree in electrical engineering.
- **Role in the chapter:** Scene 5. Co-builder of the Memory Test Computer with Taylor. His later founding of Digital Equipment Corporation (DEC) is mentioned only as a forward-pointer; the chapter's window closes before DEC's 1957 incorporation.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the Memory Test Computer attribution and his "research assistant" / "very top-notch engineer" role characterization (Forrester Concord oral history verbatim). Yellow for biographical specifics.

### Robert R. Everett (1921-2018)
- **Position in 1944-1956:** Started in the MIT Servomechanisms Laboratory in 1941 with Forrester; became Associate Director of the MIT Digital Computer Laboratory and later Associate Head of Lincoln Laboratory Division 6.
- **Role in the chapter:** Background presence in Scenes 2 and 5; Forrester's primary deputy throughout the Whirlwind / SAGE arc. With George Valley and Forrester, demonstrated the Whirlwind-based intercept-direction concept that became Project Charles' main proposal.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the deputy role and the Project Charles demonstration (Forrester Concord oral history). Yellow for biographical specifics.

## The Funder and the Catalyst

### Perry Crawford
- **Position in 1944-1946:** Engineer at the Navy Special Devices Center, Long Island; later moved to the Office of Naval Research in Washington when the Special Devices Center merged in 1946.
- **Role in the chapter:** The funding catalyst. With Forrester, decided in 1945-1946 to pivot the aircraft-stability project from analog to digital. "He was quite uninhibited and moved around to keep people focused on the possibilities of digital computers" (Forrester Concord oral history).
- **Verification:** **Green** for the analog-to-digital pivot role (Forrester Concord oral history verbatim).

### George Valley
- **Position in 1949-1951:** Associate Professor of Physics, MIT; Air Force consultant.
- **Role in the chapter:** Brought Forrester into the Air Force air-defense conversation that led to Project Charles (~1950) and ultimately to SAGE / Lincoln Laboratory.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the role linking Forrester to the Air Force air-defense study (Forrester Concord oral history).

## Co-Inventorship Dispute (Tracked as Q1)

### Way-Dong Woo
- **Position in 1949:** Research fellow at Harvard's Computation Laboratory alongside An Wang.
- **Role in the chapter:** Multiple secondary sources name Woo as co-inventor with Wang on the pulse-transfer controlling device. US Patent 2,708,722 names only Wang on the face-sheet. Until resolved (Wang autobiography or Harvard Computation Laboratory archival records), the chapter does not assert co-inventorship and tracks the dispute as `open-questions.md` Q1.
- **Verification:** Yellow — secondary attribution only. Patent face-sheet says Wang alone.

## Notes on bios

- All bios are kept narrowly to the 1947-1956 window. Later careers (Forrester's System Dynamics work; Olsen's DEC; Wang Laboratories; Rajchman's RCA executive roles) are deliberately out of scope.
- Bios mention each person's *institutional position* in the relevant year because institutional location was load-bearing for who got patents, who got contracts, and who turned a parallel invention into an industrial standard.
- No invented quotes. Where a bio implies motive (e.g., "Crawford was the funding catalyst"), the implication is anchored to a Forrester oral history quote rather than as conjecture.
- Forrester's Concord oral history is single-source; quotes from it should be attributed to Forrester's recollection, not asserted as bare third-person fact.
