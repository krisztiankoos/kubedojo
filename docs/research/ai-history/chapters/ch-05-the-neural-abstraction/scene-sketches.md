# Scene Sketches: Chapter 5 — The Neural Abstraction

The five scenes below map 1:1 to the Prose Capacity Plan layers in `brief.md`. A short epilogue inside Scene 5 carries the honest close. Do not invent scene-level dialogue or atmospherics; the verified anchors below are the upper bound on what the chapter is allowed to dramatise.

## Scene 1: The Vagabond Genius — Framed, Not Romanticised

- **Setting:** Detroit (1935 library scene, Lettvin oral history) and the University of Chicago campus (early 1940s).
- **Beat:** Walter Pitts's path from Detroit boiler-maker family into McCulloch's Hinsdale household runs through Bertrand Russell on two separate occasions: the 1935 *Principia* episode (reported only via Lettvin oral history) and the 1938 Chicago run-away to attend Russell's lectures (same chain). Lettvin introduces Pitts to McCulloch in the early 1940s. The chapter must hold its dramatic restraint here: the beats are too historically load-bearing to skip but too dependent on Lettvin's later retelling to assert as documentary.
- **Pedagogical move:** Frame the scenes explicitly as "Lettvin remembered…" or "in the version Lettvin preserved through oral history…" rather than third-person omniscient narration. Smalheiser 2000 — paywalled — would be the more measured biographical source; until physical-journal access lands, the chapter reads Gefter through Smalheiser-shaped hedges.
- **Narrative use:** Establishes Pitts as the technical capability that made the 1943 paper possible without committing the chapter to romance-isms about prodigy. The arc is "outsider with extraordinary symbolic-logic facility finds an institutional perch" — not "boy genius corrects Russell."
- **Evidence anchors:** Gefter 2015 *Nautilus* paragraph 2 (1935 library and Detroit family); paragraph 3 (3-day *Principia* read, the Russell letter, 1938 Chicago run-away); paragraph 7 (Lettvin's introduction of Pitts to McCulloch); Hinsdale paragraphs (Pitts moves into McCulloch's household). Smalheiser 2000 bibliographic anchor (paywalled, must remain Yellow). Conway-Siegelman 2005 bibliographic anchor (no page-level access yet).

## Scene 2: The Chicago Mathematical Biophysics Setting

- **Setting:** University of Chicago, early 1940s — but the scene is intellectual, not domestic. The chapter does not need a room; it needs a journal masthead, a reading list, and a community.
- **Beat:** McCulloch had been chasing a logical theory of nervous activity since his Yale and Columbia years, framed in his head as a Leibnizian "alphabet of thought" project (Gefter 2015 paragraph 7). Chicago had Nicolas Rashevsky's mathematical-biophysics group, publishing the *Bulletin of Mathematical Biophysics* — the journal that would eventually publish the 1943 paper. The biophysical assumptions about neurons (all-or-none firing, thresholds, synaptic delay) were already in the air there. What McCulloch lacked was the symbolic apparatus. Carnap's 1938 *Logical Syntax of Language* and the Russell-Whitehead *Principia* gave him a notation; Pitts gave him the technical capability to use it.
- **Pedagogical move:** This scene is the chapter's correction of the popular "first mathematical model of neurons" framing. Use Piccinini 2004's abstract claim ("in 1943 there already existed a lively community of biophysicists doing mathematical work on neural networks") and Hebb 1949 p.xv's name-list to ground the correction. The novelty being introduced in the next scene is *symbolic logic on top of biophysics*, not *mathematics applied to neurons for the first time*.
- **Narrative use:** Sets up the chapter's central distinction (continuous biophysics vs. symbolic logic) so that Scene 3's Carnap-syntax walk-through has historical weight rather than reading as pure math instruction.
- **Evidence anchors:** Piccinini 2004 abstract (Springer-anchored); Hebb 1949 p.xv (verbatim list "Rashevsky, Pitts, Householder, Landahl, McCulloch, and others"); M-P 1943 p.118 (in-text adoption of Carnap's Language II); M-P 1943 p.131 (literature list confirms Carnap 1938, Russell-Whitehead 1925, Hilbert-Ackermann 1927); Gefter 2015 paragraph 7 (McCulloch's Leibnizian project).

## Scene 3: The 1943 Paper, Read Slowly

- **Setting:** Inside the paper itself.
- **Beat:** A pedagogical walk-through of "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity" — the all-or-none assumption, the five physical assumptions of Section 2, the Carnap-syntax notation $N_i(t)$ for "neuron $i$ fires at time $t$" and the temporal-shift functor $S$, the threshold-logic gate constructions (AND at $\theta=2$; OR at $\theta=1$; NOT via inhibitory connection), and the proof that every propositional-logic expression has a corresponding net without circles.
- **Pedagogical move:** This is the math-teaching core. Reproduce one or two threshold-logic gate constructions from the paper itself; do not invent a generic toy network. The reader should leave with the ability to read the abstract claim "neural events and the relations among them can be treated by means of propositional logic" (M-P 1943 p.115) as an actual mathematical statement, not a slogan.
- **Narrative use:** The chapter earns its length here. If the reader cannot follow Scene 3 in plain prose, the chapter has failed regardless of word count.
- **Evidence anchors:** M-P 1943 p.115 (abstract); p.117 ("Many years ago one of us…" passage, mapping each neural firing to a proposition); p.118 (five physical assumptions; Language II of Carnap citation; $N_i(t)$ and the functor $S$); pp.118-124 (theorems for nets without circles; AND/OR/NOT gate constructions). All verified by `pdftotext` 2026-04-28.

## Scene 4: What the Paper Actually Proved, and Said About Learning

- **Setting:** Continuing inside the paper, into Section 3 ("Nets with Circles") and Section 4 ("Consequences").
- **Beat:** Section 3 extends the calculus to recursive predicates and bounded memory. Theorem 7 ("Alterable synapses can be replaced by circles," p.124) absorbs synaptic plasticity into fixed-connection nets with extra circular pathways — the paper's explicit, formal move on learning. The Section 1 framing paragraph (p.117) had already telegraphed this: "facilitation, extinction and learning… we can substitute equivalent fictitious nets composed of neurons whose connections and thresholds are unaltered."
- **Pedagogical move:** This scene is the chapter's defence against the flat "model has no learning mechanism" framing. The honest version is sharper and more interesting: McCulloch and Pitts *formally absorbed* learning into their fixed-net calculus — they didn't ignore it, and their move was the cleanest possible mathematical answer. What they did not provide is a *procedure* for finding the right alterations from data. That distinction (no learning algorithm vs. no learning treatment) is what unlocks the chapter's bridge to Hebb in Scene 5 and the deferral to Rosenblatt for Ch14.
- **Narrative use:** Forces the reader to read the 1943 paper as more sophisticated than the textbook compression. Sets up the precise nature of the gap that Hebb 1949 then partly fills (biologically) and Rosenblatt 1957-1958 fully fills (algorithmically).
- **Evidence anchors:** M-P 1943 p.117 (verbatim Section 1 learning paragraph); p.124 (verbatim Theorem 7 "Alterable synapses can be replaced by circles"); pp.124-130 (Section 3 development of nets-with-circles); pp.130-131 (Section 4 "Consequences" close). All verified by `pdftotext` 2026-04-28.

## Scene 5: The Hebbian Bridge — Without Calling It an Algorithm

- **Setting:** Hebb's *Organization of Behavior*, published six years later (Wiley, 1949).
- **Beat:** Hebb's Introduction (p.xv) places his work alongside, not in succession to, the Rashevsky-Pitts-McCulloch mathematical line — listing them by name as a pre-existing community. Hebb's contribution is biological: the "neurophysiological postulate" at p.62 says "When an axon of cell A is near enough to excite a cell B and repeatedly or persistently takes part in firing it, some growth process or metabolic change takes place in one or both cells such that A's efficiency, as one of the cells firing B, is increased." The chapter must read this as a *biological hypothesis* about where plasticity might live in the nervous system, not as a weight-update rule on a McCulloch-Pitts net. The textbook compression "Hebb's rule" (where weights $w_{ij}$ change in proportion to coincident activity) is a later abstraction layered on top of Hebb's biology.
- **Pedagogical move:** Be precise about what Hebb did and did not give us. He gave a biology of plasticity that the next decade of researchers would build on. He did not give an algorithm for training a McCulloch-Pitts net. The chapter must keep that line clean so Ch14 can do its job.
- **Narrative use:** Closes the chapter's main arc by naming the gap that the next two decades would try to close. Sets up Ch14 (perceptron) and the Ch24 backpropagation arc without anticipating their arguments.
- **Honest close (within Scene 5, ~300-500 words):** What 1943 actually started — Piccinini 2004's four contributions: a formalism flowing into finite automata; a technique inspiring logic design; the first computational approach to the mind-body problem; the first modern computational theory of mind and brain. Plus the von Neumann 1945 *First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC* — which cites M-P 1943 verbatim at Section 4.2, p.12 ("Following W. Pitts and W. S. McCulloch ('A logical calculus of the ideas immanent in nervous activity', Bull. Math. Bio-physics, Vol. 5 (1943), pp 115-133) we ignore the more complicated aspects of neuron functioning…") and contains no other journal citation in the entire document. Two and a half years after publication, the 1943 paper had become the singular technical foundation under one of the founding documents of digital computer architecture. What 1943 did not start: a working neuroscience program. The chapter notes — at most one sentence — that Pitts himself moved away from a strict logic-circuit reading by the end of the 1950s (the 1959 frog's-eye paper, reported via Lettvin oral history in Gefter), and ends with the chapter's stance that 1943 was a *naming of the right level of abstraction*, not a working theory of how brains learn.
- **Evidence anchors:** Hebb 1949 p.xv (community list); Hebb 1949 pp.60-62 (Chapter 4 framing; postulate at p.62 — verbatim quotation); Piccinini 2004 abstract (Springer-anchored, four-contribution honest-close); von Neumann 1945 *First Draft* p.12, Section 4.2 (verbatim citation, **Green** via Internet Archive `pdftotext` 2026-04-28); Gefter 2015 (Nautilus, frog's-eye sentence); M-P 1943 pp.130-131 (Section 4 "Consequences" close).
