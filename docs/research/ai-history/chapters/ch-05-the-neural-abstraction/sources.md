## Conflict Notes

- **"First mathematical model of neurons" framing.** Several popular accounts and the legacy Gemini-drafted Ch5 prose imply M-P 1943 was the first mathematical model of the neuron. Piccinini 2004 abstract corrects this; Hebb 1949 p.xv confirms a multi-author Chicago community already at work. The chapter must use "first symbolic-logic / first computational theory of the neuron" framing.
- **"No learning mechanism" framing.** The legacy prose says the 1943 model "had no mechanism for learning." The 1943 paper itself (p.117 and p.124, Theorem 7) does treat learning — by formal absorption into fixed-connection nets with circles. The chapter must distinguish "no learning algorithm" (true) from "no learning treatment" (false).
- **"Hebb's rule was the missing learning algorithm" framing.** Common textbook compression. Hebb 1949 p.62 is a *biological* postulate, not an algorithm; the algorithmic story belongs to Rosenblatt's perceptron in Ch14.
- **The Pitts-as-twelve-year-old anecdote.** Sourced through Lettvin oral history (*Talking Nets* 2000) and rendered in vivid prose by Gefter 2015. Smalheiser 2000 — the most careful biographical source — is paywalled. The chapter must mark these scenes as Lettvin-oral-history reconstructions and resist asserting precise dates and page counts as documentary.
- **In-text vs literature-list bibliographic dates for Russell-Whitehead.** M-P 1943 p.118 cites "Russell and Whitehead (1927)"; the literature list at p.131 cites "Russell, B. and A. N. Whitehead. 1925. Principa Mathematics." Both refer to the *Principia* second edition (whose three volumes appeared 1925-1927); the first edition is 1910-1913. Use 1925 for the cited second edition.
